# IP assignment essentials — template

Every employee, contractor, advisor, co-founder, and intern must execute IP-assignment language BEFORE they create any IP for the company. Skipping this is the single most common founder-stage legal disaster.

**Risk tier:** YELLOW for drafting; RED if you discover the assignment is missing from someone who created material IP — call counsel immediately.

---

## Why this matters

Default IP ownership rules:

- **US:** without a written assignment, IP belongs to the **creator** (with limited exceptions for "works made for hire" — and W-2 employees writing software inside the scope of employment may or may not qualify depending on circumstances).
- **EU:** varies by jurisdiction; many automatically assign employee work product to employer, but contractors are different.
- **Patents:** worldwide, inventors own patents unless explicitly assigned.

**Practical implication:** without explicit IP assignment in writing, the person who wrote that code / drew that logo / created that prototype owns it. If they leave, they can refuse to assign — or demand payment.

---

## The four populations that need IP assignment

```
1. ☐ All employees (W-2 in the US, equivalent in other jurisdictions)
   - In offer letter or separate IP/confidentiality agreement
   - Signed BEFORE start date, ideally with the offer

2. ☐ All contractors (1099 in the US, equivalent in other jurisdictions)
   - In the master services agreement OR a separate IP assignment
   - Signed BEFORE work begins
   - Most critical population — default IP ownership is the contractor's

3. ☐ All advisors (typically with equity grants)
   - In the advisor agreement
   - Signed when equity is granted

4. ☐ All co-founders
   - In a founder agreement OR a separate Technology Assignment Agreement
   - Critical: covers pre-incorporation IP (the prototype built before the company existed)
```

## Standard IP assignment language (US-style)

This is **template language for understanding**, not for direct copy-paste into a contract. Have an attorney draft your actual form.

```
INTELLECTUAL PROPERTY ASSIGNMENT

[Employee/Contractor/Advisor] hereby assigns to [Company] all right, title, and
interest in and to any and all works, inventions, discoveries, developments,
methods, processes, compositions, source code, designs, copyrights, patents,
trademarks, trade secrets, know-how, and other intellectual property
(collectively, "Work Product") that [Employee/Contractor/Advisor] creates,
authors, conceives, develops, or reduces to practice during the term of
[Employee/Contractor/Advisor]'s engagement with Company, alone or jointly with
others, that:

  (a) is created or developed during working hours or using Company resources;
  
  (b) relates to the actual or anticipated business, research, or development
  activities of Company; or
  
  (c) results from work performed by [Employee/Contractor/Advisor] for Company.

[Employee/Contractor/Advisor] agrees to execute any further documents
necessary to perfect Company's ownership rights in the Work Product, including
without limitation copyright assignments, patent assignments, and powers of
attorney for filing IP applications.

[Employee/Contractor/Advisor] hereby waives all moral rights in the Work
Product, to the maximum extent permitted by applicable law.

PRIOR INVENTIONS DISCLOSURE. [Employee/Contractor/Advisor] represents that
all inventions, original works of authorship, developments, improvements,
and trade secrets that were created or owned by [Employee/Contractor/Advisor]
prior to commencement of engagement with Company ("Prior Inventions") are
listed on Schedule A. If no list is attached, [Employee/Contractor/Advisor]
represents that there are no such Prior Inventions.
```

**Schedule A** is critical: it documents what the person came in with, so the company doesn't accidentally claim ownership of pre-existing work.

## State-specific notice (US — CA Labor Code 2870 and analogs)

In California (and several other states including Illinois, Kansas, Minnesota, Washington), the law carves out a category of inventions employees develop **entirely on their own time** without using employer resources or relating to the employer's business. The IP assignment cannot claim those.

Required notice typically reads:

```
NOTICE: Pursuant to California Labor Code Section 2870, this Assignment does
not apply to any invention developed entirely on [Employee]'s own time without
using [Company]'s equipment, supplies, facilities, or trade secret information,
except for those inventions that either: (i) relate at the time of conception
or reduction to practice to [Company]'s business or anticipated research or
development; or (ii) result from any work performed by [Employee] for [Company].
```

**Operator move:** include this notice in CA hires (and analog states). It doesn't weaken the assignment for the IP that matters; it just complies with the statute.

## Pre-incorporation IP — the founder trap

The most common IP-ownership disaster:

**Scenario:** Founder built a prototype, demo, or initial codebase for the company. Six months later, the company is formed. No IP assignment from founder to company exists.

**Result:** the founder personally owns the prototype, the demo, the initial codebase. If they leave, the company has no rights.

**Fix at incorporation:**
- Founders sign a **Technology Assignment Agreement** transferring all pre-incorporation IP to the new company
- Consideration: founder stock (this matters — the assignment needs consideration to be enforceable)
- Schedule A lists the specific pre-existing IP being assigned

**If you've already incorporated without doing this:** YELLOW immediately. Get it done now. The longer you wait, the more leverage a departing founder has.

## Contractor IP assignment — the highest-risk population

Contractors are the most-common gap. Common patterns:

| Situation | Risk |
|---|---|
| Freelance designer made your logo, no IP assignment | They own it. You have a license at best — terms unclear. |
| Contract developer wrote half your codebase, signed an MSA but no IP-assignment language in it | They own that portion of the code. Significant risk. |
| Marketing contractor wrote your website copy and produced your videos, no assignment | They own the copyright. You have an implied license but no derivative rights. |
| Offshore contractor wrote production code, the contract is vague about IP | Often unenforceable in the contractor's home jurisdiction; very high risk. |

**Operator move for contractors:**
- Use an MSA template that includes IP assignment AS A REQUIRED TERM
- Include language for both work-for-hire AND assignment (belt and suspenders — work-for-hire status varies by jurisdiction)
- Require the IP assignment to be signed before any payment is made
- For high-value contractors (developers, designers of brand assets), consider a separate IP assignment in addition to the MSA

## What to do if you discover IP assignment is missing

**Tier classification:**

| Situation | Tier |
|---|---|
| Person is still engaged, doing routine work | YELLOW — get the assignment signed now with proper consideration |
| Person is in good standing, leaving on good terms | YELLOW — get the assignment signed as part of departure; offer modest consideration if needed |
| Person has departed, no dispute | YELLOW — reach out, propose assignment for modest payment ($1-5K usually works); document the resolution |
| Person has departed and is now competing / in dispute | RED — stop. Call counsel. Anything you say or offer can be used against you. |
| Person is claiming ownership of company IP | RED — stop. Call IP counsel + litigator. Preserve all documents. |

## Audit checklist (monthly hygiene)

Run monthly until your team is large enough for HR to own this:

```
☐ Every new hire / contractor / advisor in the last 30 days has IP assignment signed
☐ All current contractors are covered (review the active vendor list)
☐ All founder agreements include pre-incorporation IP assignment
☐ All employee files have IP-assignment language (in offer letter or separate agreement)
☐ No outstanding "I'll sign that later" cases
```

---

## Hand-off triggers

Call an attorney if:

- Founder departure with material IP at issue
- IP dispute with a former employee or contractor
- Major company event (fundraise, M&A) — diligence will surface missing IP assignments
- International team and IP-assignment enforceability is uncertain
- Discovering significant IP was created without proper assignment

---

## The 30-second test

If you can't answer YES to all four in 30 seconds for any team member, you have an IP-assignment gap:

1. Has this person signed IP-assignment language with the company?
2. Was it signed BEFORE they created any IP for us?
3. Does it cover the work they actually did (not just their original scope)?
4. Was there consideration (employment, payment, equity) tied to the signature?

Any "no" or "I'm not sure" → fix it this week.
