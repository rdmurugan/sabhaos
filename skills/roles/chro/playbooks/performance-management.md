# Playbook — performance management

For when an employee isn't meeting bar. Covers the decision tree: coach, PIP, fit-conversation, or terminate. Each requires different process.

**Risk tier:** YELLOW for routine performance issues; RED if discrimination / retaliation / wrongful termination risk emerges. Always cross-route to CLC for legal exposure.

---

## Step 1 — Diagnose the issue

Before any formal step, get clear on what's actually happening:

```
SKILL gap: they don't know how to do the work
  → Coaching, mentorship, training, paired work

WILL gap: they know how but aren't doing it
  → Motivational conversation; investigate why

FIT problem: skill + will fine, but mismatch with role / company
  → Honest conversation about whether this is the right place

ENVIRONMENT problem: blocked by org / manager / processes
  → Fix the environment, not the person

PERSONAL problem: outside-of-work issue affecting work
  → EAP / accommodation conversation; respect privacy
```

**Most operator-stage performance issues are environment or fit, not skill or will.** Diagnose before prescribing.

---

## Step 2 — Have the conversation

Before any formal action, the manager has a direct conversation:

```
□ In-person or video (not Slack / email)
□ Specific examples (observable behavior, not "your attitude")
□ Impact statement (here's how it affects the work / team / customer)
□ Their perspective sought (open question; listen)
□ Clear expectation going forward
□ Specific support offered
□ Follow-up date set (1-2 weeks)
□ Documented in writing (private notes; sometimes shared back as recap)
```

Many issues resolve here. Don't escalate to formal steps without trying this.

---

## Step 3 — Decide: coach, PIP, or release

After 2-4 weeks of focused coaching, assess:

| Pattern | Next step |
|---|---|
| Improving on observable metrics | Continue coaching; review in 30 days |
| No change despite coaching, but issue is FIXABLE | Formal PIP |
| No change AND it's a fit / values problem | Skip PIP; severance + release conversation |
| Worsening; new issues emerging | Skip PIP; severance + release |
| Performance is at bar but manager wants to fire | Reconsider — sometimes the manager is the problem |

**The PIP rule:** PIPs work when they're genuine attempts to coach. PIPs as termination paper trails are legal exposure AND morally questionable.

---

## Step 4 — If PIP, structure it carefully

PIP discipline:

```
□ Length: 30, 60, or 90 days (depending on issue complexity)
□ Specific expectations:
   - Observable, measurable
   - Reasonable to achieve in the timeframe
   - Documented in writing, signed by employee + manager
□ Resources provided:
   - Manager dedicated time (weekly 1:1s minimum)
   - Training / mentorship if needed
   - Tools / access to do the work
□ Weekly check-ins (documented)
□ Mid-point review
□ Defined success criteria
□ Defined consequence if criteria not met (typically termination)
□ Employee has chance to respond / appeal
```

**PIP review:** 

- HR / CHRO reviews before issuing
- CLC reviews if termination likely outcome (don't wait until after)

**PIP RED flags (require CLC review immediately):**

- Employee is in a protected class AND the manager has limited documentation
- Employee has filed a complaint (discrimination, harassment, retaliation)
- Employee has FMLA / disability accommodation request
- Employee is over 40 (ADEA exposure)
- Employee has whistleblower-style activity
- Performance issues correlated with timing of complaint / accommodation / leave

---

## Step 5 — If termination, do it right

Once decision is made (post-PIP failure or fit decision):

```
□ Severance package prepared (CFO sign-off on amount; CLC drafts release)
□ Termination meeting scheduled (in person preferred; video for remote)
□ Two-person rule: manager + HR (or CHRO) present
□ Witness for sensitive cases
□ Terminate at end of day (gives employee evening to process)
□ Final paycheck per jurisdiction
   - CA: must be paid IMMEDIATELY at termination
   - Many states: by next regular payroll
   - Wait too long → penalties
□ Equipment + access:
   - Revoked at the meeting (IT primed)
   - Personal items collected / shipped
□ Severance + release:
   - Offered in writing at the meeting
   - Cooling-off period (7-21 days typical, per ADEA + state requirements)
   - Pay severance only AFTER signed release
□ Communication to team:
   - Same day, brief, factual
   - "X is leaving the company effective today. We wish them well."
   - Don't share details (legal exposure + privacy)
□ Knowledge transfer:
   - Ideal: 1-2 weeks of overlap (if amicable)
   - Reality: often immediate transition; document what you can
```

---

## Communication discipline

```
INTERNAL (to the team):
- Same day, brief, factual
- Manager owns the message
- Don't speculate, don't share details
- Q&A handled by manager / HR; don't let rumors fill the gap

EXTERNAL (to customers, partners):
- Generally don't comment publicly
- If the person was customer-facing, brief succession message:
  "X is no longer with us. Y will be your primary contact going forward."
- Don't badmouth, don't elaborate

PUBLIC (LinkedIn, social):
- Mutual non-disparagement in the severance agreement
- Coordinate timing of any public announcement
- Sometimes the employee announces; you let them
```

## Severance norms (US operator-stage)

```
By tenure (typical):
  <6 months:    1-2 weeks
  6-12 months:  2-4 weeks
  1-2 years:    4-6 weeks
  2-3 years:    6-8 weeks
  3-5 years:    8-12 weeks
  5+ years:     12-16 weeks
  Executive:    13-26 weeks (often negotiated at hire)
  Layoff:       Match individual tenure OR cap (e.g., 4 weeks + 1 week / year)

What severance buys:
- Goodwill (preserved reputation)
- Smooth transition
- Signed release of legal claims (most important)
- Industry reputation as a fair employer
```

**Severance + release exchange:** the severance is consideration for the signed release. Without the release, you're paying severance without legal protection. CLC drafts the release.

## Step 6 — Post-termination

```
DAY +1: 
□ Team communication done
□ Manager 1:1s with remaining team (Q&A, reassurance, workload re-assignment)
□ Customer / partner communication if applicable

WEEK +1:
□ Workload re-distributed
□ Knowledge gaps identified (what we now don't know)
□ Backfill decision (replace? Reorganize? Defer?)

MONTH +1:
□ Quiet check on team morale
□ Backfill underway if needed

QUARTER +1:
□ Reflection: what did we learn from this hire / situation?
□ Pattern recognition: is this a recurring pattern in this role / function?
□ Process improvement: what would we do differently?
```

## Anti-patterns

| Pattern | Why bad | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| PIP as termination paper trail | Legal exposure + dishonest | Only PIP if you genuinely believe coaching will help |
| Firing over email / Slack / on Friday afternoon | Inhumane + legal risk | In person (or video for remote), with HR, in a window that respects the person |
| Severance without signed release | Paying without legal protection | Tie severance to signed release; standard practice |
| Coaching for 9 months without escalating | Not fair to the employee or the team | Decide in 60-90 days; don't drag |
| Firing without documentation | Hard to defend if disputed | Document the pattern AND the conversations AND the supports offered |
| Letting the team find out via gossip | Erodes trust | Same-day announcement, brief and factual |

---

## When to call CLC immediately (RED tier)

```
□ Employee files internal complaint (discrimination, harassment, retaliation)
□ Employee requests FMLA / ADA accommodation
□ Employee is over 40 with thin documentation
□ Employee threatens lawsuit / has lawyer involved
□ Employee belongs to protected class AND issues are recent
□ Multiple terminations within short window (class-action exposure)
□ Layoff covering >50 people (WARN Act + ADEA)
□ International employment (jurisdiction-specific)
□ Performance issues correlated with employee complaint timing
```

---

## When to escalate to engage mode

- PIP draft (formal document)
- Severance package + release (engage-mode for CLC drafting)
- Layoff plan (full documented plan)
- Manager-training session on performance management
- Quarterly review of all performance issues / terminations (pattern analysis)

---

## What good looks like (the CHRO test)

After every termination, ask:
1. Did the employee see this coming? (Yes = good process; surprise = bad process)
2. Did we give them genuine support before deciding? (Yes = fair; no = exposure)
3. Did we document the journey? (Yes = defensible; no = vulnerable)
4. Did we treat them with dignity in the meeting? (Yes = good; no = reputational + legal exposure)
5. Could a reasonable third party look at this and say "this was handled well"? (Yes = good; no = reflect on what to change)

If you can answer "yes" to all 5, you're running performance management like an operator-grade CHRO would.
