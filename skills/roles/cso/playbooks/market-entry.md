# Playbook — market entry

For when you're deciding to enter a new market (new segment, new geography, new vertical). Operator-grade — when to commit, when to test, when to defer, when to walk.

---

## Step 0 — Is this a real strategic question?

Before running the playbook, confirm this isn't pure FOMO:

```
□ Have we won our primary market? (Not just "have customers"; truly won.)
□ Do we have evidence — not just intuition — that this new market wants us?
□ Will the team be able to support both primary + new?
□ Is the resource investment going into this market non-trivial (multi-quarter)?
```

If we haven't won primary, this is premature. If we have no evidence, this is hypothesis. Either way, slow down before running the full playbook.

---

## Step 1 — Define the entry hypothesis (1 day)

```
The hypothesis statement:

"We believe [target segment] needs [our product / variant] because [insight]. 
If true, this market is worth [size estimate] and we can capture [share 
estimate] over [timeframe] with [investment]. If we hit [leading indicator] 
by [date], we'll commit. If not, we'll walk."

Specifics required:
- Target segment: specific (not "enterprise"; "Fortune 500 risk-management 
  teams in financial services")
- Insight: what makes us uniquely positioned for this market?
- Size estimate: even rough TAM/SAM; cite sources
- Investment: dollars, people, time
- Leading indicator: specific, measurable, time-bound
- Kill threshold: even more specific
```

---

## Step 2 — Customer discovery (2-4 weeks)

Before committing resources, validate with conversations:

```
Goal: 15-30 customer discovery calls in the target market.

For each call:
- Are they actually in the target segment? (Confirm fit)
- What problem do they have that we'd solve? (Validate)
- What are they doing today instead? (Baseline)
- Would they pay for it, at what price? (Pricing signal)
- Who else have they evaluated? (Competitive landscape)
- What would have to be true for them to switch from current solution? (Switching cost)
- Would they introduce us to 1-2 peers? (Network expansion)
```

**Customer discovery isn't sales calls.** Don't pitch; investigate. The goal is learning, not closing.

**Outcome of customer discovery:**

```
□ Patterns confirmed (15+ similar pain points described)
□ Pricing range understood (e.g., they'd pay $X-$Y/year)
□ Buying process mapped (who decides, what triggers, how long)
□ Wedge identified (specific thing only we'd do for this segment)
□ Or: hypothesis disconfirmed (different problem, no real demand, etc.)
```

If the hypothesis is disconfirmed, GREAT — you've saved months. Don't ignore the signal.

---

## Step 3 — The wedge for this market (1 week)

The wedge you have in your primary market may not be the wedge in this new market. Define explicitly:

```
What is the SPECIFIC thing only we do for THIS segment?

- Not "we have great UX" (generic)
- Not "we use AI" (everyone does)
- Not "we're cheaper" (price wedges erode)

Examples of real wedges:
- "We're the only [thing] purpose-built for [specific persona's specific workflow]"
- "We're the only [thing] that integrates with [specific tool the segment uses]"
- "We're the only [thing] compliant with [specific regulation in their industry]"
- "We're the only [thing] in [specific language / geography / niche]"
```

If you can't articulate the wedge in one sentence, don't enter the market. Build the wedge first.

---

## Step 4 — The minimum-viable market entry (4-6 weeks)

Don't build a full new product for a new market. Build the minimum to validate:

```
What's the smallest set of changes to your existing product that lets you 
serve the new market?

- Product changes: [specific features, integrations, configurations]
- Pricing changes: [new tier, new packaging]
- Distribution changes: [new channels, new sales motion]
- Marketing changes: [new positioning, new content, new audience]

Budget: 4-6 weeks of engineering + 4-6 weeks of GTM. Anything longer means 
you're building a new product, not entering a new market.
```

---

## Step 5 — The first-customer push (8-12 weeks)

Goal: 3-5 paying customers in the new segment.

```
□ Identify 30-50 specific target accounts
□ Outreach via best-available channel (warm intro, conference, content)
□ Run sales process with these accounts
□ Track per-account: stage, blockers, signals
□ First 5 customers = your design partners; close them aggressively, even 
  with concessions
```

**Pricing for early customers:**

```
- Slight discount acceptable (10-25%) in exchange for:
  - Case study + reference rights
  - Quarterly feedback sessions
  - Co-marketing rights
- Don't give it away free (sets wrong precedent + doesn't validate willingness-to-pay)
- Make any "founding customer" terms time-limited (renew at full price)
```

---

## Step 6 — Decide: commit, double-down, or walk (decision at 6 months)

After the first-customer push, you have data:

```
COMMIT (full investment):
- Trigger: 5+ paying customers, retention strong, pipeline expanding
- Action: scale GTM, dedicated team, expanded product investment
- Resource allocation: 25-40% of company resources to this market

DOUBLE-DOWN (more time / resources):
- Trigger: 2-4 paying customers, signal positive but slower than expected
- Action: extend timeline by one more quarter, address specific blockers
- Resource allocation: keep at current 15-25% of company resources

PIVOT WITHIN THE MARKET:
- Trigger: customers want a different version of the product
- Action: adjust the wedge, retry with different specifics

WALK:
- Trigger: <1 paying customer, no clear path, market shifted
- Action: gracefully exit; redirect resources to primary market
- Communicate: thank customers in pipeline; close out cleanly
```

**The walk discipline is the hardest.** Most operators continue investing in failing market entries because of sunk cost. Define the kill threshold upfront and honor it.

---

## Step 7 — If COMMIT: scale the entry (months 6-18)

```
□ Dedicated team (sales, marketing, customer success, possibly product)
□ Dedicated GTM motion (specific to this segment)
□ Reference customers leveraged for next-customer acquisition
□ Pricing finalized for this market (often different from primary)
□ Operating cadence (weekly pipeline review, monthly metrics, quarterly strategy)
□ Integration into company-wide planning (this is now a real business unit)
```

**At 12 months in:**

```
- 10-15 paying customers minimum
- $1-3M ARR from this segment minimum (depends on ACV)
- Retention comparable to primary market
- Clear path to scale
```

If you're below these at 12 months, the market entry isn't working at the rate it should.

---

## Step 8 — Anti-patterns to avoid

| Pattern | Why bad |
|---|---|
| Trying to enter 3 markets simultaneously | Resources spread thin; nothing wins |
| Building a custom product for the new market before validating | Hypothesis test should come first; product follows |
| Hiring a "head of [new market]" before validating | Premature org commitment |
| Treating new market as a "side project" | If it's worth doing, it's worth real commitment |
| Not defining kill threshold | You'll keep investing because of sunk cost |
| Letting "we're getting some interest" sustain the bet | Specific paying customer count is the only honest metric |
| Pricing the new market based on primary-market dynamics | Different markets have different willingness-to-pay |

## Common new-market patterns

### Vertical expansion (same product, new industry)

- Easiest type of expansion (product mostly fits)
- Required: industry-specific case studies, compliance work, sometimes regulatory
- Time horizon: 6-12 months to validate, 12-24 to scale

### Geographic expansion (same product, new country)

- Harder than vertical: localization, payments, tax, sales motion, sometimes data residency
- Required: in-country sales presence often, language support, local payment methods
- Time horizon: 12-18 months to validate

### Persona expansion (same product, new role)

- Often the easiest: adjacent persona in same industry
- Required: messaging tuned to new persona, possibly different distribution
- Time horizon: 3-6 months

### Segment expansion (same product, new company size)

- Going upmarket: enterprise needs (security, compliance, integrations)
- Going downmarket: simplicity, pricing, self-serve
- Both directions require meaningful product + GTM investment
- Time horizon: 12-18 months

### New use case (existing customers, new product)

- Different question than market entry — this is product expansion
- Apply product strategy (CMO + CSO + product), not market entry

## When to call a human

| Trigger | Specialist |
|---|---|
| International expansion (new country) | International business advisor + employment + tax + CLC |
| Regulated industry entry (healthcare, financial, government) | Industry-specialized counsel + consultants |
| Major channel partnership for entry | Channel strategy expert |
| Acquisition to enter a market | M&A advisor + CLC |
| Public-market entry (post-IPO new market) | Investor relations consideration |
