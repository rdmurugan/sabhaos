For your primary market. Build once, refresh quarterly. Operator-grade — don't over-engineer.

## The 2x2 positioning map

Pick the two dimensions that matter most for buyer choice in your market. Common dimensions:

```
Possible axes:
- Price: low ←→ premium
- Capability: narrow ←→ broad
- Segment focus: SMB ←→ enterprise
- DIY ←→ done-for-you
- Generalist ←→ specialist
- Cloud ←→ on-prem
- Speed ←→ depth

Pick the 2 that map to how buyers actually choose.
```

```
              [Axis 1: e.g., Broader capability]
                          ▲
                          │
                   [Quadrant A]    [Quadrant B]
                          │
[Axis 2: lower price] ◄───┼───► [Axis 2: premium]
                          │
                   [Quadrant C]    [Quadrant D]
                          │
                          ▼
              [Axis 1: e.g., Narrower capability]
```

Place each competitor in its quadrant. Place your company. Place your **intended** position (where you're aiming).

## Competitive frame for your story

After mapping competitors, write your competitive story:

```
"In a market that includes [Competitor A] and [Competitor B], 
[my company] is different in [specific dimension]. 

[Competitor A] focuses on [their angle]; that's good if you need [X], but 
they don't [our wedge].

[Competitor B] is best for [their target]; that's good if you fit, but they 
don't [our wedge].

We're the choice when you need [your specific wedge value]."
```

This becomes the sales talk-track + the marketing positioning + the analyst-brief talking points. One consistent story.

## Competitive intelligence (what to track, lightly)

Per competitor, track quarterly (15-30 min each):

```
□ Pricing page changes
□ Major product announcements
□ New partnership announcements
□ Notable customer wins / losses
□ Team changes (especially executive)
□ Fundraising / financial news
□ Customer reviews trend (G2, Capterra, Trustpilot)
```

**Don't track exhaustively.** This is a check-in, not a research project.

## The win/loss interview discipline

Per quarter, run 2-3 win/loss interviews:

```
WON DEAL — interview the customer who picked you:
- Who else were you considering?
- What were the deciding factors?
- What almost made you pick someone else?
- What were the close calls?

LOST DEAL — interview the customer who picked someone else:
- Who did you pick?
- What were the deciding factors?
- What would have made you pick us instead?
- Was price the main issue, or something else?
- Any feature gaps you noticed?
```

**Win/loss interviews are the highest-signal competitive intelligence.** They beat any amount of competitor research. Skip the research; do the interviews.

## When competitors don't matter

Sometimes the framework's wrong because the answer is "competitors don't really matter here":

- Pre-PMF stage: focus on product-market fit, not competitors
- Greenfield categories: there are no competitors yet (just substitutes / status quo)
- Highly specialized niches: customers care about your specific fit, not category comparisons
- Network-effect markets: the leader's lead is structural, not competitive-feature-based

**Use the competitor map when buyers compare. Don't use it when buyers just decide.**

---

## Anti-patterns

| Pattern | Why bad |
|---|---|
| 30 competitors on the map | Focus on the 3-5 buyers actually consider |
| Detailed feature-by-feature comparison matrices | Buyers don't choose this way; outcomes matter |
| Updating the map weekly | Quarterly is enough; weekly is procrastination |
| Reading too much into one competitor's announcement | Wait for patterns |
| Competitor map drives product roadmap | Your roadmap should be customer-driven, not competitor-driven |
| Treating competitors as enemies vs. resources | Compete vigorously, respect competitors. They're often customers / partners later. |

## When to call a human

| Trigger | Specialist |
|---|---|
| Major M&A by a competitor (consolidating your space) | Strategic advisor + board |
| Pricing collapse by a major competitor | Strategic advisor — sometimes this signals structural change |
| New entrant with significant funding (>$50M) | Investor / strategic advisor consult |
| Persistent loss to a competitor (>50% of competed deals) | Sales / GTM expert + product input |
| Industry analyst (Gartner, Forrester) is positioning competitor above you | Analyst-relations consultant if it's material |
