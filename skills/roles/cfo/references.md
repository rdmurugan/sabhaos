# CFO — references & further reading

Citations for the frameworks referenced in `REFERENCE.md`, `heuristics.md`, and the playbooks/templates. Where I could not cite a specific article reliably, I cite the *body of work* and the originator.

This is curated, not exhaustive. The frameworks listed have all proven durable across decades of operator practice.

---

## Decision-making and behavioral finance

- **Kahneman, D.** *Thinking, Fast and Slow.* Farrar, Straus and Giroux, 2011. The canonical text on System 1 / System 2 reasoning, anchoring, availability, planning fallacy, loss aversion. Every cognitive bias quick-catch in `heuristics.md` traces back here or to the underlying Kahneman & Tversky papers.

- **Kahneman, D., Lovallo, D., & Sibony, O.** "Before You Make That Big Decision." *Harvard Business Review*, June 2011. The 12-question pre-decision checklist for major commitments. Inspires the engage-mode memo discipline.

- **Klein, G.** "Performing a Project Premortem." *Harvard Business Review*, September 2007. The premortem method — imagining a future failure to surface present-day risks. Used in `playbooks/fundraise-prep.md` and `templates/capital-allocation-matrix.md`.

- **Tversky, A. & Kahneman, D.** "Judgment under Uncertainty: Heuristics and Biases." *Science*, 1974. The original mapping of anchoring, availability, and representativeness heuristics.

- **Thaler, R.** *Misbehaving: The Making of Behavioral Economics.* W.W. Norton, 2015. Sunk-cost fallacy and mental accounting; particularly relevant to the cost-cut playbook.

## Strategy and competitive positioning

- **Porter, M.** "How Competitive Forces Shape Strategy." *Harvard Business Review*, March 1979. Five Forces — competitive analysis canon. Referenced as the substrate for pricing competitive analysis.

- **Hamel, G. & Prahalad, C.K.** "Strategic Intent." *Harvard Business Review*, May 1989. The long-horizon strategic framing that the CSO/CEO role draws on; relevant to fundraise narrative ("why now, why this").

## Capital allocation and corporate finance

- **McKinsey & Company.** *Valuation: Measuring and Managing the Value of Companies* (Koller, Goedhart, Wessels). Wiley, multiple editions. The reference text on capital allocation discipline, NPV/IRR methodology, and the long-arc literature on how value gets created or destroyed by allocation choices. Underpins REFERENCE §4 (capital allocation matrix).

- **Brealey, R., Myers, S., & Allen, F.** *Principles of Corporate Finance.* McGraw-Hill. The standard finance text. NPV, real options, capital structure.

- **Myers, S.** "Determinants of Corporate Borrowing." *Journal of Financial Economics*, 1977. Origin of real options theory in corporate finance. Relevant to the reversibility frame in cost-cut and capital allocation decisions.

## SaaS and unit economics

- **Skok, D.** *forEntrepreneurs* blog (forentrepreneurs.com). The most widely-cited operator canon on SaaS unit economics — LTV, CAC, payback, cohort retention. Referenced throughout REFERENCE §2 and `templates/unit-economics.md`.

- **Sacks, D.** "Burn multiples" — popularized as a measure of capital efficiency for SaaS via blog posts and podcasts. Underpins REFERENCE §1.

- **Mamoon Hamid & Byron Deeter** and the broader Bessemer Venture Partners "State of the Cloud" reports — the source for Rule of 40 framing as the dominant public-market lens.

## Pricing

- **Ramanujam, M. & Tacke, G.** *Monetizing Innovation.* Wiley, 2016. The value-based pricing canon — building price into product design rather than bolting on at launch.

- **Bertini, M. & Koenigsberg, O.** Various HBR pieces on pricing strategy. Useful for tier design and willingness-to-pay reasoning.

- **Kahneman, D. & Tversky, A.** Prospect theory papers — origin of the asymmetry between gain and loss perception that informs how customers respond to price changes (loss aversion on price increases).

## Operations and cost management

- **Bain & Company.** Published operator notes on cost-restructuring (cost-cut playbook tracing back to Bain's "results delivery" methodology). The STARK ordering in `playbooks/cost-cut-decision.md` is in the spirit of Bain's reversibility-first cost frameworks.

- **Bezos, J.** Amazon shareholder letters — particularly the 1997 letter and the 2015 "two-way doors" framing. Public letters; freely available at sec.gov filings. The reversibility frame appears in `REFERENCE.md` §8 and across the playbooks.

## Scenario planning

- **Wack, P.** "Scenarios: Uncharted Waters Ahead." *Harvard Business Review*, September 1985. The original Shell scenario planning methodology — multi-scenario thinking under genuine uncertainty. Underpins `templates/runway-model.md` §"Three scenarios."

- **van der Heijden, K.** *Scenarios: The Art of Strategic Conversation.* Wiley, 2005. The operator-friendly elaboration of Wack's method.

## Performance measurement

- **Kaplan, R. & Norton, D.** "The Balanced Scorecard — Measures That Drive Performance." *Harvard Business Review*, January 1992. The four-perspective scorecard framework. Generalized for early-stage operators in REFERENCE §11.

## Founder/operator perspectives (book-length)

- **Horowitz, B.** *The Hard Thing About Hard Things.* HarperBusiness, 2014. Operator-grade decision-making under pressure; particularly relevant to cost-cut and layoff playbooks.

- **Andreessen, M., Horowitz, B., et al.** a16z published articles on the SaaS metric stack and unit economics. Particularly useful for board-grade narrative on metric direction.

- **Wilson, F.** *AVC* blog (avc.com). Long-running operator/investor commentary, with archived posts dating back to 2003 covering capital allocation, fundraise dynamics, and cost discipline.

---

## A note on attribution

The CFO body of work has multiple parallel canons — academic finance (Brealey/Myers), management consulting (McKinsey, Bain), behavioral economics (Kahneman/Tversky/Thaler), operator literature (Skok, Wilson, Sacks), and corporate finance practitioners (CFO Magazine, McKinsey Quarterly). Where a concept appears in multiple traditions, I've cited the earliest or most influential rather than every iteration.

For specific URLs or article access, search the title at the publication's website (hbr.org, mckinsey.com, bain.com, forentrepreneurs.com). I have not embedded URLs because I cannot verify their current availability — direct search is more reliable than dead links.
