# Worked example 01 — SaaS repositioning

**Scenario:** Founder positions as "the AI productivity tool for solo founders." Signups: 200/month. Conversion to paid: 3%. Sales calls reveal most signups think the product is "a generic ChatGPT wrapper." Question: reposition or fix the funnel?

Walks the full CMO diagnostic + JTBD canvas + repositioning playbook applied to a real decision.

---

## Step 1 — Triage (`heuristics.md` §10-second triage)

```
Problem type:    Positioning, message, distribution, or product?
Evidence?:       Founder has sales-call evidence ("they think we're a ChatGPT wrapper")
Funnel stage?:   Top of funnel (acquisition message) → conversion (3% paid)
```

The founder *has* evidence — sales calls. That's good. The question is whether the evidence points to positioning, message, or something else.

## Step 2 — Apply the diagnostic from `playbooks/repositioning.md` Step 0

```
□ Customer interviews: do customers describe the product matching the positioning? → Need data; assume no based on the "ChatGPT wrapper" signal
□ Inbound quality: do prospects understand without explanation? → No (the ChatGPT wrapper confusion proves this)
□ Sales calls: do customers describe in product's words or theirs? → In neither (vague)
□ Win-rate by segment: clear "we win when we're for X" pattern? → Unclear from the scenario
□ Lost-deal language: what do they say when they don't buy? → "It's just a ChatGPT wrapper"
```

3+ of these point to positioning. **Proceed with repositioning consideration**, but don't commit until customer interviews validate.

## Step 3 — Why is "AI productivity tool for solo founders" failing?

Diagnose:

| Dimension | Read |
|---|---|
| **Category** | "AI productivity tool" — this is exactly the category where ChatGPT wrappers live. Buyers' default category assumption IS "ChatGPT wrapper" because that's what 90% of AI productivity tools are. |
| **Target** | "Solo founders" — reasonable, but the category is so generic that the target gets diluted |
| **Benefit** | Implicit ("productivity") — not concrete enough to fight off the wrapper assumption |
| **Differentiation** | Missing — there's no "unlike [X]" in this positioning |

The core failure: the category language is putting the product into a default mental bucket that's commoditized. **The fix isn't "more marketing"; it's a different category claim.**

## Step 4 — Run customer language audit (week 1-2 of playbook)

Hypothetically, the founder runs 20 interviews (10 customers, 5 lost-deal, 5 prospects). Patterns emerge:

| Theme | Frequency | Customer language |
|---|---|---|
| "I'm tired of getting 5 options to consider" | 8/20 | "I just need it to decide." "Stop hedging." |
| "I need it to know my context" | 6/20 | "It forgets everything I told it last week." "Why doesn't it remember my projects?" |
| "It's the closest thing I have to a real advisor" | 5/20 | "It's like having a CFO in my terminal." |
| "AI productivity / ChatGPT wrapper" | 4/20 | (the wrong-category signal — confirms the diagnostic) |
| "I work alone; this is my team" | 7/20 | "I'm a one-person company." "There's no one else to ask." |

The strongest *push*: option-shaped AI replies are exhausting.
The strongest *pull*: a decisive advisor that knows you.

**The repositioning material is in the customer language.**

## Step 5 — Three draft positionings (`templates/positioning-statement.md`)

### Draft A — narrow target, sharp category

```
For solo founders running one-person companies, 
Sabha is a local-first AI council 
that turns Claude into a decisive executive advisor with memory of your decisions.

Unlike generic AI assistants that give you five options to consider,
Sabha forces a routing decision and a recommendation with a named tradeoff.
```

### Draft B — same target, new category framing

```
For founders who can't afford a real C-suite,
Sabha is the protocol that turns Claude into your CFO, CMO, and CIO 
in one terminal command.

Unlike consulting or fractional hires,
your Sabha council is always available, costs nothing per call, 
and learns your business over time.
```

### Draft C — adjacent target, broader appeal

```
For anyone running something — a company, a side project, or a life decision —
Sabha is a local-first AI council that gives you decisive recommendations 
with the trade-offs named.

Unlike AI assistants that give you options and leave the decision to you,
Sabha forces a role-based answer that compounds into your locally-stored 
Sakthi (your accumulated knowledge).
```

## Step 6 — Pick the right draft

Apply the five tests:

| Test | Draft A | Draft B | Draft C |
|---|---|---|---|
| Category test (clear comparison?) | "Local-first AI council" — distinctive | "CFO/CMO/CIO in terminal" — vivid but conflates with consulting | "AI council" — distinctive but broad target |
| Customer test (matches their words?) | High match — "decisive advisor with memory" | Medium match — they don't call it "C-suite" themselves | Medium match — broad target dilutes |
| "Unlike" test (specific, verifiable?) | Strong — "five options" is the actual pain point | Strong — costs nothing is verifiable | Strong — routing + tradeoff is verifiable |
| Polarization test (clear yes/no?) | Yes — solo founders ARE clearly target | Mixed — non-founders self-deselect | No — "anyone running something" doesn't polarize |
| Repeat test (commit 2 years?) | Yes | Maybe — the C-suite framing might feel limiting |  Maybe — too broad to defend |

**Draft A wins** on 4 of 5 dimensions. Go with it.

## Step 7 — Validate (week 5 of playbook)

Show Draft A to 5 fresh prospects who haven't seen current marketing. Ask:

> "What do you think this is for? Who would buy this? Who wouldn't?"

Predicted responses:
- "Solo founders" — direct quote from the statement, confirms target
- "Someone who's frustrated with how AI hedges" — confirms the push lands
- "Not for a team / big company" — confirms the polarization works

If 4 of 5 prospects describe it correctly and self-identify as ICP or non-ICP, the positioning lands.

## Step 8 — The recommendation

```
Routing: CMO. CXO weighs in on the conversion-funnel implications.

This is a positioning problem, not a funnel problem. Fixing the funnel without 
fixing positioning would compound the wrong direction — more signups thinking 
you're a ChatGPT wrapper.

Recommendation: Reposition from "the AI productivity tool for solo founders" 
to "a local-first AI council for solo founders." The category change is the 
move.

Reasoning:
- "AI productivity tool" puts you in the ChatGPT-wrapper bucket. The category 
  itself is your problem.
- "Local-first AI council" creates a new category. Buyers compare you to 
  fractional CFOs and advisors, not to AI assistants. That's a 10× better 
  comparison set.
- Customer language confirms: "decisive advisor with memory" is what they 
  actually want. Match the language.

Tradeoff:
- You lose 6 months of compounding on the old positioning. Anyone who knew 
  you as "the AI productivity tool" has to relearn.
- The new category is unfamiliar; you'll spend the first 90 days explaining 
  "local-first AI council" — but that's a feature, not a bug. Distinctive 
  categories take effort but compound.
- Some current signups (the wrong-fit ones) will self-deselect. That's also 
  a feature.

Concrete next moves (follow `playbooks/repositioning.md`):
1. This week: 10 customer interviews. Use the question set in §1 of the playbook.
2. Weeks 1-2: draft the new positioning statement; iterate on customer language.
3. Week 3: internal alignment — sales, product, founder all signed on.
4. Weeks 4-6: rollout per the playbook §6 sequence.
5. Weeks 7+: measure per the 30/60/90 framework.

What to watch (weeks 1-12):
- 30-day: outbound reply rate, inbound message quality, "what do you do" answers
- 60-day: demo-to-close ratio, new-customer descriptions of you
- 90-day: paid conversion rate, brand search volume

Kill criterion: at 90 days, if paid conversion is materially WORSE than the 3% 
baseline (say, <2%), the new positioning isn't landing. Investigate and 
potentially iterate (rare to fully revert; usually means a tweaked version 
is the answer).

When to call a human:
- IP / trademark check on "Sabha" / "Sakthi" / "Chanakya" before broadcasting 
  publicly (likely fine but verify with counsel)
- If the repositioning becomes a launch event (PR / press), the messaging 
  needs PR-firm review

This is engage-mode work — file the positioning brief and the rollout plan 
as documents. Want me to write the positioning brief now?
```

## Step 9 — What made this answer good

| Dimension | Why it works |
|---|---|
| Diagnosed correctly | Positioning, not funnel — and explained why |
| Framework-cited | Customer language audit (playbook); five positioning tests (template) |
| Concrete language | Specific old vs new statements with reasoning |
| Tradeoff explicit | 6 months compounding lost; new category requires teaching |
| Polarization defended | Wrong-fit deselection is a feature |
| Cross-role | CMO primary, CXO advisory on funnel impact |
| Hand-off named | Legal (IP/trademark), PR (if launch event) |
| Mode escalation offered | Positioning briefs are engage-mode artifacts |

## Step 10 — What the bad version would look like

A weaker CMO would have answered:

> "Sounds like a messaging issue. I'd recommend a website refresh with a clearer hero, some A/B testing on the headline, and maybe a tighter ICP. Try LinkedIn ads if you haven't yet — the targeting is great for founders. Also, NPS surveys would help you understand customer perception."

What's wrong:
- Doesn't diagnose (positioning vs message vs distribution)
- Recommends every tactic instead of the one move that matters
- "Website refresh" is the classic non-answer
- "Try LinkedIn" without channel hypothesis
- NPS doesn't fix positioning
- No tradeoff, no kill criteria, no leading indicator

The Chanakya tradition rejects "try everything." Diagnose, recommend, accept the tradeoff.

## Variants

| If… | Then… |
|---|---|
| Customer interviews showed they DO describe you correctly | Problem isn't positioning; investigate funnel mechanics (message or product) |
| Lost-deal interviews showed a product gap, not message gap | Don't reposition; fix the product gap |
| Current customers love you but new prospects don't get it | Sharpen ICP, don't reposition; you have fit, just bad targeting |
| Repositioning would put you in a more competitive category | Verify the math: does the better positioning beat the more crowded category? Sometimes yes, sometimes no |
| You've repositioned in the last 12 months | Don't reposition again. Make the current positioning work for 24 months minimum |
