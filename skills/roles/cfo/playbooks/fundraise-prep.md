# Playbook — fundraise prep

Start this **6 months before** you need the cash. Earlier if you're at an inflection (pre-PMF, pre-revenue, post-pivot). The "money you don't need" doctrine: best terms go to founders who don't visibly need to close.

---

## Phase 1 — Position (T-6 to T-4 months)

```
□ Get unit economics into investable shape
    LTV/CAC ≥ 3 (or trajectory clearly arriving at it)
    CAC payback < 12 months for SaaS
    NRR > 100%
    Gross margin > 70% for SaaS
□ Get 3-6 months of clean, consistent metrics
    Pick the cohort definition you'll use in the deck and stick to it
    Make sure trailing metrics are auditable
□ Build the "why now" narrative
    The wave you're catching — what changed in the world
    The forced choice the buyer faces — pain that justifies switching
    Why this couldn't have been done 3 years ago
□ Get a credible reference customer in each ICP segment
    Willing to take an investor call
    With at least 6 months of usage data
□ Refresh the cap table — clean any aged options, vested founder shares, etc.
```

**Honest check:** if the unit economics aren't where they need to be, the answer isn't a better deck. The answer is delaying the raise by a quarter while you fix them. Investors smell mid-tier metrics with a top-tier deck.

---

## Phase 2 — Prepare the materials (T-4 to T-2 months)

### The deck (10-12 slides max)

```
1. Title — company name, tagline, your name, contact
2. Mission / why now — the wave
3. Problem — what's broken for whom, with evidence
4. Solution — what you built and the wedge
5. Traction — numbers, with a 6-month trajectory
6. Business model — pricing, gross margin, unit economics
7. Market — TAM/SAM/SOM with reasoning, not just numbers
8. Competition — positioning map, with your defensibility
9. Team — why this team, gaps you'll close with funds
10. The ask — round size, use of funds, milestones it unlocks
11. (Optional) Roadmap — next 18 months of bets
12. (Optional) Appendix — diligence-ready
```

### The data room

```
□ Financials — last 3 years (or since inception), monthly
□ Cap table — current, with option pool details
□ Customer cohort data — retention by cohort, NRR by cohort
□ Pipeline — current pipeline by stage, with deal-level confidence
□ Org chart — current and pro-forma post-raise
□ Contracts — top 10 customer contracts, key vendor contracts
□ IP — assignments from contractors and former employees
□ Corporate docs — incorporation, board minutes, prior round docs
□ HR — handbook, employee agreements, contractor agreements
```

### The narrative spine (rehearsed)

Practice answering these in 90 seconds each:

```
□ Why you, why this, why now?
□ What's the wedge — the thing only you can do?
□ Where will you be in 18 months with this money?
□ What's the biggest risk you see?
□ Who else are you talking to and why them?
□ What's the smallest amount that lets you hit your next milestone?
□ What happens if you don't raise?
```

If you can't answer #4 (biggest risk) and #7 (no-raise plan) cleanly, you're not ready.

---

## Phase 3 — Run the process (T-2 to T-0 months)

### Build the target list

```
□ 20-30 investors total
□ Tiered: top 5, mid 15, long-tail 10
□ Researched: each firm's last 5 investments in your space
□ Warm intro path identified for each top-tier
□ Sequencing planned: open with mid-tier (practice, learn objections); top-tier in middle; long-tail at end
```

**Why mid-tier first:** you'll get objections. You want those objections to surface and be patched *before* you sit in front of the top-tier you actually want.

### The week-by-week

| Week | Action |
|---|---|
| W-8 | Send first-meeting deck to 5 mid-tier; book first meetings |
| W-6 | First meetings; patch objections; refine deck |
| W-4 | Send to top 10 (top-tier + best mid-tier); push for partner meetings |
| W-2 | Partner meetings; offer term sheets requested |
| W-0 | Term sheets received; negotiate; sign |
| W+2 | Diligence; legal |
| W+6 | Close, wire received |

### Negotiating term sheets

When you have one offer:
- Don't sign immediately. Take 48 hours minimum.
- Tell other active investors you have a term sheet (truthful pressure).
- Push on: valuation, board composition, liquidation preference, anti-dilution, pro-rata.
- Yield on: minor governance terms that don't materially shift control.

When you have two offers:
- Negotiate term sheets against each other on the dimensions that matter to you.
- The firms know they're competing; the better firm will often improve terms unprompted.

When you have zero offers after 6 weeks:
- The deal is broken. Don't keep pitching the same deck to more investors.
- Diagnose: is the market wrong (timing), the deck wrong (positioning), the metrics wrong (substance), or the team wrong (gap)? Each requires a different fix.

## Phase 4 — After the round closes

```
□ Update the cap table
□ Set the board meeting cadence (quarterly minimum; monthly for first 6 months)
□ Pay down any bridge / convertible obligations
□ Settle any deferred-comp that paused for the raise
□ Communicate to the team (specific, with milestones)
□ Update memory: who invested, on what terms, with what specific signals to track
```

**The post-close memo to yourself:** what did you commit to investors that they'll ask about in 6 months? Write it down. Make sure each commitment has a leading indicator you're tracking.

---

## Anti-patterns

| Anti-pattern | Fix |
|---|---|
| Starting at <4 months runway | Costs you 20-30% of valuation. Always start at T-6 months. |
| Pitching every investor the same deck | Each tier has different concerns. Customize the "why now" per investor. |
| Hiding weakness | Diligence will find it. Pre-empt in slide 8; you control the framing. |
| Optimizing for highest valuation | Wrong objective. Optimize for best investor at fair valuation. The investor matters more than the price. |
| Closing without checking references on the investor | Talk to 2-3 founders they've backed (including one whose company didn't work). |
| Mistaking interest for commitment | "We're interested" isn't a term sheet. Don't update your runway plan until you have signed terms. |

---

## When to abort the round

You should pause or abort if:

- After 4 weeks and 15 meetings, you have zero partner meetings scheduled. The deal isn't landing; diagnose.
- The terms on offer have liquidation preference >1.5× or aggressive anti-dilution. Cheaper to bootstrap.
- Investors are demanding a pivot. If you believe the pivot is right, take it; if not, walk.
- The market shifts mid-raise (rates spike, sector falls out of favor). Sometimes pausing 90 days is the right move.

A failed raise isn't fatal. A bad raise locks in bad terms and bad partners for 5-10 years.
