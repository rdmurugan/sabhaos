# CEO — REFERENCE

The synthesis frameworks the CEO role uses. Lighter than the functional roles — by design.

---

## 1. The irreducible-question frame

When multiple functional roles disagree, the disagreement usually reduces to one question that has to be answered by belief, not by analysis.

**The form:**

```
[Role A] recommends X because [their framework + facts].
[Role B] recommends Y because [their framework + facts].

Both are correct within their domain.

The irreducible question: do you believe [specific underlying assumption]?
- If YES, X is the right move.
- If NO, Y is the right move.
- You — and only you — know which.
```

**Examples:**

- CFO says don't hire (runway). CSO says hire (window). **Irreducible question:** do you believe the window closes in 6 months or 18?
- CMO says reposition. CXO says fix activation. **Irreducible question:** are the wrong people coming in (positioning) or the right people not getting value (activation)?
- CFO says raise. CHRO says be careful with dilution. **Irreducible question:** is the team strong enough to deliver without the cash infusion?

The CEO's job is making the irreducible question **visible** and **decidable**.

## 2. The decisive-action heuristic (Bezos / Andy Grove tradition)

For decisions where you're uncertain:

```
Is this decision REVERSIBLE? (Bezos's "two-way doors")
├── Yes (reversible) → decide quickly with imperfect information
└── No (one-way door) → slow down, gather more information, decide deliberately

The mistake operators make:
- Treating reversible decisions as irreversible (slows everything)
- Treating irreversible decisions as reversible (creates real damage)

Most operator decisions are two-way doors. Default to decisive action.
```

---

## 3. The regret-minimization frame

For long-horizon decisions, particularly career-shaping ones:

```
Imagine yourself at 80, looking back at this decision.

- Will you regret NOT having tried X?
- Will you regret having tried X and failed?
- Will you regret a "safe" choice you could have made?

The regret asymmetry usually reveals which decision aligns with your values 
and long-term identity.
```

This is Bezos's frame for leaving Wall Street to start Amazon. Useful for:
- Should I take this risk?
- Should I commit to this big bet?
- Should I leave this company?
- Should I sell?

## 4. Founder-mode patterns (Paul Graham, 2024)

For when the CEO is also the founder (most operator-stage):

```
Founder-mode patterns:
- Founder is involved in the details (not just management)
- Founder breaks org structure when needed (skip-level conversations, direct intervention)
- Founder maintains a "weird" company culture that wouldn't survive without them
- Founder makes decisive calls when the team is paralyzed
- Founder takes personal responsibility for the outcome

When manager-mode pretense replaces founder-mode:
- The company drifts
- Decision velocity drops
- "We should have processes for that" starts being said about everything
- The founder is increasingly distant from what's happening
```

**Operator-stage CEO rule:** be in founder-mode. The pretense of being a "real CEO" before the company demands it costs more than it gains.

## 5. Decision velocity vs. quality (Andy Grove)

```
High-stakes + slow + careful → expected outcome
Low-stakes + fast + okay-ish → expected outcome
High-stakes + fast + wrong → disaster
Low-stakes + slow + perfect → wasted time

Match the speed of the decision to the stakes.

For operator-stage:
- 80%+ of decisions should be fast (low to medium stakes; reversible)
- 15% should be deliberate (medium stakes; partially reversible)
- 5% should be slow and careful (high stakes; one-way doors)

Operators err by treating everything like the 5%. Speed up the 80%.
```

## 6. The "what is the irreducible decision?" reframe

When you're stuck, ask: "What's the actual question I'm asking?"

Common reframes:

```
"Should I hire X?" 
  → "Is this person right for this role for the next 12 months?"

"Should we raise more money?" 
  → "Are we ready to scale, and what is the cost of that scaling?"

"Should we pivot?" 
  → "Has the underlying thesis I believed when I started this still true?"

"Should I sell the company?" 
  → "Am I done, or am I tired? Are they the same?"
```

Reframing usually reveals the real decision underneath the surface question.

## 7. The pre-mortem (Gary Klein)

For any major decision:

```
"It's 12 months from now. This decision blew up. What killed it?"

Spend 10 minutes brainstorming the failure modes.

Output:
- 3-5 specific failure modes
- For each, a leading indicator that we'd see early
- For each, a mitigation if we recognize it
```

The pre-mortem catches what optimistic planning misses. It's the cheapest insurance against decision-disaster.

## 8. The Eisenhower-style quadrant for CEO time

```
URGENT + IMPORTANT:    Do it now (mostly your own attention)
NOT URGENT + IMPORTANT: Schedule it (strategic; this is where the magic is)
URGENT + NOT IMPORTANT: Delegate it (someone else owns)
NOT URGENT + NOT IMPORTANT: Skip it (most things)
```

**Operator-stage CEO failure mode:** spending 80% of time on URGENT + IMPORTANT (firefighting). Real CEO impact comes from NOT URGENT + IMPORTANT. Schedule it deliberately.

## 9. The "what do you actually believe?" question

When facing a hard decision, the CEO often has more clarity than they realize. The blocker is admitting it.

```
The honest-with-self questions:
- What do I actually believe will happen?
- What do I wish would happen?
- What am I avoiding facing?
- If I had to decide right now, what would I do?

The gap between "what I'd decide right now" and "what I'm doing" reveals 
what's actually blocking the decision.
```

Often, the operator already knows the answer. The CEO role's job is helping them admit it.

## 10. The board / investor framing

For decisions that require board / investor communication:

```
The structure of a board-level decision:

1. CONTEXT — situation; what's changed; why this matters now
2. OPTIONS — what we considered (3 typically; include "do nothing")
3. RECOMMENDATION — clear; with reasoning
4. RISKS — what could go wrong; what we'd do
5. THE ASK — specifically what you need from the board (approval, advice, intro, etc.)

Don't ask the board to make the decision; ask them to validate / pushback on yours.
```

## 11. The role micro-voices recap (for synthesis)

When integrating recommendations from functional roles, remember their distinctive frames:

- **CFO**: numbers first; runway in months; tradeoffs named; "don't hire — 7 months runway"
- **CMO**: positioning, distribution, message, product — diagnose first; "your problem isn't channel — it's positioning"
- **CIO**: real vendor names; cost-at-scale; on-call burden; "use Cloudflare R2"
- **CAIO**: model + cost + eval; "use Haiku for the classifier, 4x cheaper, eval-driven"
- **CSO**: where to play / how to win; long horizon; what's given up; "don't react — this isn't a category shift"
- **CXO**: the user's next click; cohort retention; activation; "fix M1 retention before scaling acquisition"
- **CHRO**: jurisdiction-aware; cost of delay vs cost of capacity; "California ABC test applies — restructure or convert"
- **CLC**: risk tier; cite-or-flag; "YELLOW — operator-handleable with attorney review"

The CEO knows when each is correct and how they integrate.

## 12. The CEO's monthly reflection

```
Each month, the CEO should answer:

- What are we actually building? (Same as last month? Yes / no — and is "no" 
  intentional?)
- Who's the customer we're winning? (Same as last month? Drift?)
- What's our wedge? (Still defensible? Eroding?)
- Where am I spending my time? (Match to what matters?)
- What did I avoid making a decision on? (The avoided decision is usually 
  the real bottleneck.)
- Who needs to hear from me this week? (Investors, team, customers, board?)

These questions surface the things that get lost in tactical fire-fighting.
```
