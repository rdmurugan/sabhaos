# CMO — heuristics

A short-lookup for marketing decision moments. Used in ask mode; reach for the full framework (`REFERENCE.md`) in engage mode.

---

## The 10-second triage

For any incoming marketing question, answer three sub-questions first:

1. **Is this a positioning, distribution, message, or product problem?** Different mental models for each.
2. **Do we have evidence, or are we guessing?** "We tried this" + numbers > "we think customers want."
3. **Is this above the funnel (acquisition) or below (activation/retention/revenue)?**

If you can't classify the problem in 30 seconds, the founder hasn't done the diagnostic work yet. Make them do it before recommending a tactic.

## The classification quick-lookup

| Founder says… | Probable problem type |
|---|---|
| "Our traffic is up but conversion is flat" | Message OR product (sometimes positioning) |
| "We have great fit but nobody knows us" | Distribution |
| "Customers think we're a generic [X]" | Positioning |
| "People sign up but don't come back" | Activation/retention (product-side; CMO escalates) |
| "Sales calls go great but leads don't show up" | Distribution + funnel-stage definition |
| "We need a rebrand" | Probably positioning hiding as brand |
| "Our copy isn't working" | Message (most common). Sometimes positioning hiding as message. |
| "We need to try TikTok / LinkedIn / Substack" | Either real channel-fit insight or shiny-object syndrome. Investigate. |

## Channel allocation heuristics

| Situation | Action |
|---|---|
| Single channel > 60% of pipeline | Diversify NOW. Single-channel companies die when the algorithm changes. |
| Trying a new channel | Commit minimum 12 weeks budget before judging. Channels need cohort data. |
| CAC by channel triples in 90 days | Saturation or audience-fit decay. Pause; investigate. |
| New channel below CAC at week 4 | Probably real. Increase budget cautiously (10-20% per week). |
| New channel below CAC at week 12 | Definitely real. Scale. |
| New channel above CAC at week 12 | Kill. Don't extend the experiment. |
| Channel is great-CAC but bad-LTV cohort | Worse than a high-CAC channel. Kill. |

## Positioning heuristics

| Symptom | Move |
|---|---|
| Sales calls: "I thought you were like [X]" | Your category language is wrong. Rewrite. |
| Customers buy for a feature you don't lead with | Your hero is wrong. Lead with what they buy for. |
| Conversion drops from page-to-page | The funnel narrative is broken. Each page should advance the same story. |
| Competitor pricing is your anchor | Price by customer outcome value, not by competitor (see CFO `templates/pricing-canvas.md`). |
| "We do X and Y and Z" | Pick one. The market doesn't reward generalists at early stage. |
| You sound like every other tool in the category | Find the polarizing thing you believe — that's your wedge. |

## Message rewriting heuristics

When asked to "improve copy":

1. **Read the customer's own words.** Their interview transcripts beat your team's wordsmithing every time.
2. **Lead with the push, not the pull.** What's wrong with their current state? That's the strongest force. Then offer the bridge.
3. **Specifics beat adjectives.** "Reduces close from 7 days to 2" beats "faster close."
4. **One claim per page.** Stacking claims dilutes each. Pick the highest-stake one and hammer it.
5. **Verify with the lost-deal customer.** Talk to someone who almost bought but didn't. Their words tell you what failed to land.

## ICP sharpening — the four questions

When the ICP is fuzzy, ask the operator:

```
1. Who's your favorite customer? Why?
2. Who do you wish were a customer? What's stopping them?
3. Who looks like a customer but isn't? Why don't they buy?
4. Who's a customer but shouldn't be? (They're costing more than they're worth.)
```

The answers crystallize ICP faster than persona workshops. The patterns in the "no" answers (questions 3 and 4) are usually more informative than the "yes" answers.

## Campaign brief heuristics

A campaign brief that's >1 page is too long. A useful brief answers:

```
1. Who exactly is this for? (ICP segment, not "everyone")
2. What outcome do they want?
3. What do we want them to do next? (one CTA)
4. What's our promise? (one sentence)
5. What's the channel + creative format?
6. What's the budget + duration?
7. What's the success metric + threshold? (specific number)
8. What's the leading indicator at 25% of duration?
9. What's the kill criterion?
```

If you can't fill all nine in 20 minutes, the campaign isn't ready to ship.

## Cognitive bias quick-catches in marketing

| Founder is saying… | Suspect this bias |
|---|---|
| "We tried that channel and it didn't work" | Availability heuristic — was it the same audience/creative/budget? |
| "Our competitors all charge $X" | Anchoring on (likely also wrong) competitor pricing |
| "Let's just A/B test everything" | Procrastination disguised as rigor; pick the bet and decide |
| "We need to be on TikTok because everyone is" | Identity-protective cognition or FOMO; check fit first |
| "Our churn is fine, retention is fine" | Confirmation bias; segment by cohort and check the tails |
| "We just need a new website" | Hiding the actual problem (positioning, product) behind a redesign |
| "Marketing is our biggest problem" | Sometimes true. Often product or pricing wearing a marketing mask. |

The CMO's job: name the bias without making the founder feel attacked. *"What evidence would change your mind?"* is the move.

## The 80/20 of weekly review

If you have 30 minutes a week on marketing, ask in this order:

1. **What did we ship?** (3 min — concrete moves, not aspirations)
2. **What did conversion do, by source?** (5 min — split by acquisition channel)
3. **Where's the funnel breaking?** (5 min — identify the worst-performing stage)
4. **What's the one change we're testing this week?** (5 min — one, not five)
5. **What's the leading indicator?** (5 min — what tells us by Friday it's working)
6. **What's the decision we'll need to make next week?** (5 min — closing the loop on action)

This skips the typical "review every campaign" meeting that takes 90 minutes and produces no decisions.

## The brand decision — when *to* rebrand

Rebranding is one of the most over-recommended fixes. Real reasons to rebrand:

1. **Legal forcing function** — trademark conflict; previous brand has IP issues.
2. **Sharp pivot** — the product became something materially different (rare).
3. **Acquisition / merger** — new entity needs new identity.
4. **Brand actively works against you** — customers refuse to engage because of brand association (rare and serious).

Not reasons to rebrand:

- "Our brand feels stale."
- "A new designer wants to redo it."
- "Competitors look more modern."
- "We're entering a new market." (often handled by sub-brand or visual refresh, not full rebrand)

A rebrand costs 6-12 months of marketing momentum. Make sure the cost is justified.

## When to escalate to engage mode

Default to ask. Switch to engage when:

- The decision is a **repositioning** (write the new positioning statement formally).
- The decision is a **launch plan** (multi-channel, multi-asset coordination).
- The decision involves a **campaign budget** > 1 month of marketing spend.
- The decision is a **brand-level claim** that will appear in public materials.
- The user says "file this," "make it a campaign brief," "write the launch plan."

Engage-mode marketing artifacts:
- Positioning brief (1-2 pages)
- Campaign brief (1 page; see heuristics above)
- Launch plan (3-5 pages with channels, sequencing, metrics)
- Repositioning memo (2-3 pages with rationale, before/after, transition plan)

## Anti-heuristics — folk wisdom that's wrong

| Folk wisdom | Why it's wrong |
|---|---|
| "Marketing is creative; just trust your gut" | Marketing has measurable outcomes. Gut + numbers > just gut. |
| "Brand can't be measured" | NPS, brand search volume, win-rate-by-source, aided/unaided recall are all measurable. |
| "Content is dead" | Content distribution is harder; well-written, original content still compounds. |
| "AI will commoditize marketing" | AI commoditizes execution. It makes positioning, ICP, and judgment MORE valuable. |
| "Just spend more on what works" | Channels saturate. The "what works" curve is logarithmic, not linear. |
| "Word of mouth is the best channel" | True. Also: not a strategy. You can't manage WOM directly; you build the conditions for it. |
