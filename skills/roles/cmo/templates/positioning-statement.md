# Positioning statement — template

The single most important marketing artifact. Get this right and every other decision is downstream of it; get it wrong and every other decision compounds the wrong direction.

---

## The canonical positioning statement (Trout & Ries, sharpened)

```
For [target customer segment]
who [statement of need or opportunity that they have],
our [product / service]
is a [product category]
that [statement of key benefit / compelling reason to buy].

Unlike [primary competitive alternative],
our product [statement of primary differentiation].
```

Fill it in. Read it out loud. Then test it against the questions below.

---

## Test 1 — The category test

```
Q: When you say "a [product category]," what does the buyer compare you to in their head?
```

If the answer is "AI tool" or "SaaS platform" — your category is too broad. Buyers can't position you because the comparison set is huge.

If the answer is "the [specific kind of thing] that does [specific kind of job]" — you have a workable category.

**The fix when category is too broad:** narrow until you can name 3-5 specific competitors. If you name "everyone in [huge category]," you have no category.

---

## Test 2 — The customer test

```
Q: Could you walk this statement up to 5 customers and have them agree it describes them and what they bought?
```

If the customers shrug or rephrase it — your positioning is wrong relative to their actual purchase reasoning.

The fix: **interview 5 customers about why they bought.** Use their words. The right positioning is in their language, not yours.

---

## Test 3 — The "unlike" test

```
Q: Is the "unlike" comparison genuine? Is the differentiation true AND verifiable?
```

Common failures:
- "Unlike enterprise tools, ours is easy to use." → Every tool says this. Not differentiating.
- "Unlike competitor X, we have AI." → Now every AI tool is your competitor. You traded a specific differentiation for a generic one.
- "Unlike everyone else, we focus on [specific user]." → Possibly real. Verify: do your top customers actually fit that "specific user"?

The fix: name a *specific* competitor and a *specific* observable difference.

---

## Test 4 — The polarization test

```
Q: Does this positioning create a clear "this is for me / this is not for me" reaction in 3 seconds?
```

Bad positioning is universally palatable; nobody is excluded, but nobody is excited either. Good positioning polarizes: the right buyer thinks "this is exactly for me," the wrong buyer thinks "this isn't for me." Both reactions are valuable.

The fix: if the statement could describe 5 different products, it's too generic. Add a specific dimension.

---

## Test 5 — The repeat test

```
Q: Will you actually repeat this language for the next 2 years?
```

Positioning compounds with repetition. If you can't see yourself saying this same sentence at every sales call, on every landing page, in every blog post — you'll abandon it in 6 months, and the compounding never happens.

The fix: choose a positioning you're willing to commit to for 24 months minimum.

---

## After the test — three-week distribution plan

Once the positioning passes all five tests, integrate it:

```
Week 1:
□ Homepage hero rewritten — exact positioning language
□ "About" page rewritten
□ One-line bio in all founder profiles
□ Email signature
□ First slide of every deck

Week 2:
□ All sales call openers
□ Every social profile (LinkedIn, X, etc.)
□ Content calendar — next 4 posts use the language
□ Outbound email templates

Week 3:
□ Internal team rallying — every team member can recite it
□ Customer-facing materials (help docs, onboarding)
□ Investor deck
□ Job descriptions (this is the company you're hiring into)
```

If the new positioning isn't visible in every customer touchpoint within 3 weeks, the change isn't real.

---

## Leading indicators (30/60/90 days)

| 30 days | 60 days | 90 days |
|---|---|---|
| Sales call qualifier hit-rate (do leads sound like ICP?) | Demo-to-close ratio | Inbound message volume |
| "How did you find us / what made you reach out" answers | New-customer NPS | Customer language convergence (do new customers describe you the same way?) |
| Outbound reply rate | Pipeline by ICP segment | Brand search volume (your name as a query) |

If 60 days in the inbound quality metrics haven't moved, the positioning isn't landing. Investigate (channel? execution? wrong positioning?).

## Worked example

**Before (generic):**

> For modern teams, our platform is an AI-powered productivity tool that helps you work better. Unlike traditional tools, we use AI to make your workflow seamless.

What's wrong: vague target ("modern teams"), vague category ("AI-powered productivity tool"), vague benefit ("work better"), vague differentiation ("AI"), vague "unlike" (against what?).

**After (positioned):**

> For solo founders running one-person companies, our CLAUDE.md protocol is a local-first AI council that turns Claude into a decisive executive advisor. Unlike generic AI assistants that give you five options, Sabha forces a routing decision and a recommendation with a named tradeoff — so you stop getting surveys and start getting calls.

What's better: specific target ("solo founders running one-person companies"), specific category ("local-first AI council"), specific benefit ("decisive executive advisor"), specific "unlike" ("generic AI assistants that give you five options"), specific differentiation ("routing decision + recommendation + tradeoff").

A buyer in the target reads this and knows in 5 seconds whether it's for them. A buyer outside the target also knows it's not for them. That's the polarization that drives conversion.

## When to re-do positioning

Don't reposition more than once every 18-24 months. Real triggers:

- Customer interviews reveal the buying reason has shifted (rare; usually means the market has matured).
- A new category has emerged that's a better fit (e.g., "DevOps" → "Platform Engineering").
- Your product has genuinely become a different thing.
- You're entering a new ICP segment that requires its own positioning (often handled by a sub-page, not a full reposition).

Anti-triggers (do NOT reposition for these reasons):
- "It feels stale."
- "Marketing wants something fresh."
- "Our competitor changed theirs."
- "We have a new VP of Marketing."

The cost of repositioning is 6-12 months of compounding lost. Make sure the reason is structural.
