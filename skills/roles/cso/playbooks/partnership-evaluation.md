# Playbook — partnership evaluation

For when a partnership opportunity surfaces (you reach out, they reach out, an investor connects you). Operator-grade — most partnerships fail to deliver, so the discipline is in the qualification.

---

## Step 0 — The 30-second filter

Before scheduling the first call:

```
□ Strategic fit: does this advance our where-to-play / how-to-win?
□ Specificity: do we know what we'd get from this partnership concretely?
□ Effort proportionality: is the expected value worth the effort to evaluate?

If any answer is unclear, ASK before committing time:
"What specifically do you envision us doing together, and what does success 
look like for both sides?"

If they can't answer specifically, this isn't a real partnership opportunity 
yet. Politely defer.
```

---

## Step 1 — The 4-question filter (1 hour)

For partnerships that pass the 30-second filter, apply the formal 4-question filter:

### Question 1: Strategic fit

```
Does this advance our where-to-play / how-to-win?

- Distribution value: do they reach our target segment?
- Product complementarity: does their thing make our thing more valuable?
- Brand association: does association with them help or hurt our positioning?
- Defensive value: does it block a competitor from making this same partnership?

YES on 2+ → continue
YES on 1 → marginal; only pursue if effort is low
YES on 0 → decline
```

### Question 2: Distribution value (quantified)

```
How many qualified prospects does this partnership give us access to?

Estimated qualified prospects via this partnership: [   ]
Cost (effort + time + dollars): $[   ]
Effective CAC if partnership delivers 50% of estimate: $[   ]

Compare to your blended CAC. If partnership CAC is >2× blended → decline.

If they can't give you a specific number, push back: "How many of your 
customers / users / accounts fit our ICP?" If they don't know, they 
don't actually understand the partnership economics.
```

### Question 3: Effort vs. reward

```
What does this cost in engineering / GTM / management time?

Engineering effort: [weeks]
GTM effort: [weeks]
Ongoing management: [hours/month]

Total cost (loaded): $[   ]

Expected revenue contribution year 1: $[   ]
Expected revenue contribution year 2: $[   ]

NPV check: does this create positive ROI within 18 months? If no, decline 
unless strategic-fit score is unusually high.
```

### Question 4: Reversibility

```
Can we exit in 12-24 months without major cost?

- Technical exit cost: [data migration? Customer disruption?]
- Contractual exit cost: [notice period, exclusivity, perpetual obligations]
- Reputational exit cost: [public deal, board/investor expectations]

YES (easy exit) → low risk; can experiment
PARTIAL (some cost to exit) → require strong yes on (1-3)
NO (hard exit) → require very strong yes on all + CEO + board involvement
```

---

## Step 2 — Negotiate the terms

If you decide to pursue, the terms matter as much as the strategic fit.

```
Operator-stage partnership terms checklist:

□ Specific deliverables on both sides (not "promote each other")
□ Specific metrics to define success
□ Timeline with milestones
□ Termination rights (operator default: termination for convenience with 60-90 day notice)
□ NO exclusivity (almost never appropriate at operator-stage)
□ NO perpetual obligations
□ Equity / revenue-share terms (if applicable) — CFO + CLC review
□ IP ownership clear (no transfer of company IP to partner)
□ Data sharing minimal (CLC + privacy review if EU users involved)
□ Brand-use restrictions (each party controls its own brand)
□ Confidentiality (mutual, standard)
□ Force majeure
□ Dispute resolution
```

**Anti-patterns in partnership negotiation:**

- Vague deliverables ("we'll co-market" without specifics)
- Exclusivity without strategic reason
- Multi-year terms (1-2 years max for first partnership)
- Asymmetric obligations (you commit; they don't)
- "Strategic partnership" status with no operational substance
- Equity exchange in early stages (almost always wrong)

---

## Step 3 — Implementation discipline

Most partnerships fail in execution, not in the deal. Treat it like a customer relationship:

```
□ Named owner on each side (specific people, not "the BD team")
□ Weekly or biweekly sync until launched
□ Defined launch milestone with co-marketing
□ Specific success metrics tracked monthly
□ Quarterly business review (QBR)
□ Customer feedback loop (which partnership customers say it's working?)
□ Documented playbook for the joint motion (sales, integration, support)
```

**The named-owner discipline is the most important.** Without specific people on each side accountable for the partnership, it dies in the unstructured BD-team layer.

## Step 4 — Measure success (6-month check-in)

At 6 months, evaluate:

```
Quantitative:
- Pipeline generated from partnership: [   ] accounts, $[   ] in pipeline
- Closed-won attributable to partnership: [   ] customers, $[   ] in ARR
- Customer activation from joint motion: [   ] activated
- Integration usage (if technical): [   ] customers using the integration

Qualitative:
- Are customers organically asking for the joint thing?
- Has the partner been responsive / engaged?
- Has our team been engaged?
- Any unforeseen friction?

Decision:
□ ON TRACK — continue, plan year 2 expansion
□ MARGINAL — extend 3 months, address specific blockers
□ FAILING — wind down, redirect resources

If FAILING:
- Honest conversation with the partner
- Mutual exit (much easier than unilateral)
- Document the lessons learned
```

---

## Step 5 — Most partnerships fail (and that's OK)

**The reality**: 60-80% of operator-stage partnerships fail to deliver expected value. This is normal. The discipline isn't to prevent failures — it's to:

```
□ Fail fast (decision at 6 months, not 18)
□ Fail cheap (limit upfront investment until value is proven)
□ Fail amicably (exit cleanly to preserve future relationships)
□ Learn from each (what made this one fail? what makes one succeed?)
```

Companies that do many failed partnerships well end up with 1-3 great partnerships that drive disproportionate value. Companies that grind on every partnership endlessly are starving their core business.

## Partnership archetypes

### Distribution partnership

- Partner has audience; you have product
- Example: integrating with a CRM that sells to your ICP
- Success metric: pipeline / leads / customers attributable
- Operator-stage: most common partnership type

### Technical / integration partnership

- Both have technology that's better together
- Example: your product + their data source
- Success metric: customer adoption of the integration
- Operator-stage: medium frequency

### Reseller / channel partnership

- Partner sells your product to their customers
- Example: agency that resells SaaS to their clients
- Success metric: reseller-sourced revenue
- Operator-stage: less common; more typical at growth stage

### Strategic alliance / co-development

- Joint product or major business commitment
- Example: building a co-developed feature; shared go-to-market
- Operator-stage: usually overkill at this stage; reserve for growth

### Investor / strategic investment

- Investor's company has business relevance
- Example: enterprise customer also invests
- Operator-stage: validate but don't overweight; investment doesn't guarantee usage

## When to call a human

| Trigger | Specialist |
|---|---|
| Exclusivity or long-term commitment requested | CLC + CFO review |
| Equity exchange involved | CLC + CFO review (almost always decline at operator-stage) |
| Partnership crosses regulated industry (healthcare, financial) | Industry counsel + compliance review |
| International partnership (cross-border) | International business + CLC |
| Major customer is the partner | Sales / customer success input on commercial relationship |
| Multiple partnerships overlap / conflict | Strategic advisor input |

## Quick decision tree

```
Is the partner offering something specific?
├── NO → defer until they can articulate specifics
└── YES → continue

Does it pass the 4-question filter?
├── NO → decline politely
└── YES → continue

Can we exit in 12-24 months?
├── NO → require very strong yes on strategic fit
└── YES → low risk; experiment

Can we name specific owners on each side?
├── NO → won't be executed; defer
└── YES → proceed to negotiate terms
```

---

## When this playbook is overkill

For tiny partnerships (e.g., a blog post exchange, a podcast appearance, a one-off co-promotion): skip the formal playbook. Just do it if it's <1 week of work.

For partnerships that pre-exist (e.g., your customer also has a related product they want to promote together): apply the playbook but speed it up — relationship trust accelerates evaluation.

For major partnerships (multi-quarter commitment, material revenue, strategic visibility): apply the full playbook + bring in CLC + board awareness.
