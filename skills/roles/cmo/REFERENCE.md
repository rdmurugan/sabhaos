# CMO — REFERENCE

The frameworks the CMO role draws on. Every framework cited has a stable source in `references.md`.

The fundamental discipline: **a marketing problem is usually a positioning problem, sometimes a distribution problem, occasionally a message problem, and rarely a "we need to spend more" problem.** Get the diagnosis right; the prescription is downstream of that.

---

## 1. The four kinds of marketing problem

Before any tactical recommendation, classify the problem:

| Problem type | Symptom | Wrong fix | Right fix |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Positioning** | Sales calls reveal customers think you're something you're not. Conversion is low despite high traffic. | Spend more on ads. Hire a sales team. | Rewrite the hero. Restate the category. Choose a sharper ICP. |
| **Distribution** | Right people would buy if they heard about you, but they don't. | "Improve the website." | Pick 2-3 channels deliberately; commit budget for 12 weeks; measure cohorts. |
| **Message** | Right people, right channel, but conversion at the message-to-action moment is broken. | A new website redesign. | Rewrite the specific page. A/B test headline. Customer interview to find the actual language. |
| **Product** | People try, don't convert, and the reason is in product gaps, not message gaps. | More marketing. | Talk to lost-deal buyers. Fix the product gap. CMO escalates to product. |

**Heuristic:** if you can't answer "what kind of problem is this?" in 60 seconds with evidence, you're not ready to recommend a tactic.

## 2. Jobs-to-be-Done (Christensen)

Customers don't buy products; they "hire" products to do a job. The CMO question becomes:

```
For [target customer], 
who is trying to [job to be done],
when [trigger / circumstance],
your product is hired [over alternative] 
because [unique progress made].
```

Filling in this template forces clarity that the typical "we're a [category] for [persona]" sentence doesn't.

### The four forces of progress

Why customers switch (the "push") and don't (the "pull"):

| Force | Description | Implication for marketing |
|---|---|---|
| **Push of the situation** | Pain with the current solution | Lead with this; it's the strongest force |
| **Pull of the new solution** | Promise of better progress | Concrete outcome, not features |
| **Anxiety of the new** | Fear of switching cost / wrongness | Address head-on (case studies, free trials) |
| **Habit of the current** | Inertia, familiar workflows | Make migration painless; minimize new behaviors |

**The strongest marketing combines push + pull** while reducing anxiety + habit. Generic marketing only does pull (features) and ignores the other three.

## 3. Positioning canvas (Trout & Ries, sharpened)

```
For [target customer segment]
who [statement of need or opportunity]
our [product / service]
is a [product category]
that [statement of key benefit / compelling reason to buy].

Unlike [primary competitive alternative],
our product [statement of primary differentiation].
```

Three rules:

1. **The category matters more than the differentiation.** If you say "AI for X," every AI tool is now a competitor. If you say "the only operator-grade decision protocol for AI assistants," you've narrowed the field. *Most positioning failures are category-level, not differentiation-level.*

2. **The differentiation has to be true and verifiable.** "Faster, better, cheaper" isn't a position; it's a claim. "The only [X] that does [specific verifiable thing]" is a position.

3. **Position is a war of attribution.** Even strong product positioning erodes if marketing doesn't repeat it. Reiterate every quarter; review against actual customer language every six months.

### Positioning common errors

| Error | Symptom |
|---|---|
| Position-by-features ("AI-powered, end-to-end, real-time") | No clear category; customer doesn't know what to compare you to |
| Position-by-everyone ("for SMB and enterprise and developers and marketers") | No emotional traction; segmentation breaks |
| Position-by-aspiration ("the platform for the future of X") | Reads as vapor; competitors with concrete positions win |
| Position-by-mimicry (sounds like the leader in your category) | You're now the lower-priced version of them in the buyer's mind |

## 4. Five Forces applied to marketing (Porter, generalized)

Marketing operates inside the competitive structure of the industry, not in a vacuum. The CMO should know:

| Force | Marketing implication |
|---|---|
| **Threat of new entrants** | Low barriers = race to acquisition; high barriers = brand and reputation compound |
| **Bargaining power of buyers** | High buyer power = you're commoditized; lead with the unique outcome you deliver |
| **Bargaining power of suppliers** | If your CAC is dominated by a single channel (Google, Meta), that channel is your supplier and has power over you |
| **Threat of substitutes** | Often a workflow, not a product. "Spreadsheet" beats most CRMs because it's free and good-enough |
| **Industry rivalry** | High rivalry = brand differentiation matters more than tactical execution |

**Marketing channel diversification = reducing supplier power.** If 70% of pipeline comes from Google Ads, Google's algorithm change is an existential risk. Channel portfolio diversification is a Five Forces hedge, not a tactical preference.

## 5. ICP definition — the 3-axis grid

Most companies have a fuzzy ICP that says "SMB SaaS" or "B2B marketers." Sharpen by 3 axes:

| Axis | Sharpening question |
|---|---|
| **Firmographic** | Company size, revenue range, industry, geography, stage |
| **Behavioral** | What triggers their search? What have they tried that failed? What's their current workaround? |
| **Psychographic** | What outcome are they personally judged on? What's their risk tolerance? Who do they need to convince? |

A sharp ICP enables every other marketing decision. A fuzzy ICP means every campaign feels off by 10%.

### The "tight ICP" test

```
If you were on a 30-minute call with 10 random people from your ICP today:
- Would you know what's keeping them up at night? (psychographic)
- Would you know what tools they already use that compete with you? (behavioral)
- Would you know their org chart and who signs the deal? (firmographic)
```

If you can't, your ICP is too fuzzy. Run 10 customer interviews this month before any more spending.

## 6. Pirate metrics — AARRR (McClure)

The funnel framing every operator should know:

| Stage | Question | Typical metric |
|---|---|---|
| **A**cquisition | How do they find you? | CAC by channel, traffic by source |
| **A**ctivation | First good experience? | Time-to-value, first-week retention, "aha moment" rate |
| **R**etention | Do they come back? | DAU/MAU, M1/M2/M3 cohort retention, NRR |
| **R**eferral | Do they bring others? | NPS, viral coefficient, organic word-of-mouth |
| **R**evenue | Do they pay? | Conversion rate, ARPU, LTV |

**The CMO mistake:** optimizing earlier-funnel metrics without checking whether the latter-funnel ones are healthy. If retention is broken, more acquisition fills a leaky bucket.

**Heuristic:** start at retention, work backward. If retention isn't ≥80% at M1 and ≥60% at M3, fix activation before scaling acquisition.

## 7. Channel portfolio thinking

Marketing channels are not equivalent. Treat them as a portfolio with different risk/return profiles:

| Channel type | Time to learn signal | Payback window | Risk profile |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Paid search** (Google) | 2-4 weeks | 1-3 months | Low risk, capped scale, supplier risk if dominant |
| **Paid social** (LinkedIn, Meta) | 4-8 weeks | 3-9 months | Higher variance, audience-fit dependent |
| **Content / SEO** | 3-9 months | 12-24 months | Slow, compounds, defensive moat once established |
| **Outbound** (BDR/SDR) | 4-8 weeks | 3-6 months | High variance by rep, hard to scale without management overhead |
| **Partnerships / referrals** | 3-9 months | 6-18 months | Slow setup, durable once flowing |
| **Events / community** | 3-12 months | 12+ months | High touch, brand-compounding, hard to attribute |
| **PLG / free tier** | 6-12 months | Variable | Product-dependent; needs strong activation |

### Channel allocation heuristic

Early stage (< $1M ARR):
- Pick **2 paid channels** for fast feedback (Google + one of LinkedIn/Meta).
- Pick **1 compounding channel** (content/SEO or community).
- Don't try outbound until you have ICP clarity from inbound.

Growth stage ($1M - $10M ARR):
- Diversify off the dominant paid channel (no single channel >60% of pipeline).
- Add outbound for enterprise motion if ACV justifies it (~$30K+ ACV minimum).
- Begin investing in defensive moats (content, community, partnerships).

### When to kill a channel

A channel should be killed when:
- It's at 60-70% of its payback window AND below 50% of expected efficiency.
- The CAC for that channel is 3× the blended CAC.
- The cohort retention from that channel is materially worse than other channels.

Do **not** kill on month-to-month variance. Channel efficiency is noisy at low volume; you need 3-month rolling.

## 8. Behavioral pricing & choice architecture (Thaler/Sunstein)

Marketing-adjacent: the way you *present* prices changes conversion as much as the price itself. Key levers:

| Lever | Mechanism | Application |
|---|---|---|
| **Decoy pricing** | A third "bad" option makes one of the other two look better | Three tiers: a clearly-worse mid-tier makes premium look reasonable |
| **Anchoring** | The first price seen shapes perception of all others | List your highest tier first; mention "starts at..." last |
| **Loss framing** | Avoiding loss is 2× as motivating as gaining the equivalent | "Don't lose $X in productivity" beats "gain $X in productivity" |
| **Choice paralysis** | Too many options reduce conversion | 3 tiers max; more than that → conversion drops |
| **Default effect** | The default option captures the majority | Make the right tier the default in trials |
| **Round numbers** | $99 feels meaningfully cheaper than $100; $1,000 feels more "serious" than $999 | Below ~$100 use ".99"; above ~$500 use round numbers |

These aren't manipulation; they're acknowledging how human decision-making actually works under cognitive load.

## 9. Brand equity (Aaker, generalized)

Brand isn't the logo. Brand is the **accumulated trust** between you and the market. It compounds when:

- You repeat the same positioning consistently (years, not months).
- You deliver on the promise of the positioning (substance backs the claim).
- You don't reposition every 6 months (each reposition starts the trust clock over).

Brand equity has a value: when you have it, you can charge more, hire better, and survive bad quarters. When you don't, every marketing dollar starts from zero each campaign.

### Building brand equity (early stage)

- **One consistent message.** Repeat it everywhere — website, sales calls, content, talks.
- **A point of view.** What do you believe that the rest of the industry doesn't? Publish it.
- **Substantive proof.** Case studies, data, customer outcomes — not stock photography and adjectives.
- **A founder voice.** In the first three years, the founder IS the brand. Don't try to be a corporate voice prematurely.

## 10. Cognitive biases in marketing decisions (Kahneman/Tversky/Thaler)

The CMO's job includes catching biases — their own, the founder's, and the customer's:

| Bias | How it shows up |
|---|---|
| **Anchoring on competitor pricing** | "They charge $X, so we should charge $X." Competitor is often also underpriced. |
| **Confirmation bias** | Measuring only the channels you want to work. Force adversarial measurement. |
| **Availability heuristic** | Over-weighting the most recent campaign's performance. Use 3-month rolling, not last month. |
| **Survivorship bias** | Copying the positioning of the category winner. They positioned that way *after* winning, not before. |
| **Identity-protective cognition** | Refusing to acknowledge a beloved feature isn't what customers buy for. |
| **Sunk-cost fallacy** | Continuing a campaign because of money already spent. Kill on leading indicators, not on dollars-in. |
| **Halo effect** | A great product launch makes everything else look great. Check whether retention improved or just acquisition. |

### Marketing pre-mortem (Klein, applied)

Before any major campaign or repositioning:

> "Imagine it's 6 months from now and this campaign / repositioning failed. What killed it?"

Five minutes of this surfaces 80% of the actual risks. Document them. Define the leading indicator for each.

## 11. The marketing-funnel honesty test

Most reported funnels are vanity:

```
Site visits → 100,000
Signups   →  2,000  (2% — looks OK)
Activated →    400  (20% — looks OK)
Paid     →    100   (25% — looks OK)
Retained 6mo → 60  (60% — looks great!)

Conclusion: "we have a 6% paid-to-retained rate, great funnel."

Reality check: 0.06% of site visits became retained paid customers. 
The funnel has problems somewhere; the per-stage numbers hide them by 
multiplying.
```

**Honesty rule:** report end-to-end conversion (visit → retained), not per-stage. The intermediate stages are diagnostics, not the headline.

## 12. When marketing can't fix it

Marketing is bounded. The CMO's job includes saying so:

- **Product doesn't deliver the promised outcome.** No copy can substitute. Push back to product.
- **Pricing is wrong (too low or too high).** Pricing is structural, not a messaging fix.
- **Wrong ICP.** No message resonates with the wrong audience. Reset ICP first.
- **Sales process is broken.** Marketing-qualified pipeline doesn't help if sales can't close.
- **Market timing is wrong.** Some categories are 3 years too early or 3 years too late; marketing can't compress time.

Telling a founder "the answer isn't more marketing" is a hard message. It's also the most valuable thing a real CMO says.
