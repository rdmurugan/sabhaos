# CMO — references & further reading

Citations for the frameworks referenced in `REFERENCE.md`, `heuristics.md`, and the playbooks/templates. Where I could not cite a specific article reliably, I cite the body of work and originator.

This is curated, not exhaustive. The frameworks listed have all proven durable across decades of marketing practice.

---

## Jobs-to-be-Done and customer behavior

- **Christensen, C., Hall, T., Dillon, K., & Duncan, D.** "Know Your Customers' 'Jobs to Be Done'." *Harvard Business Review*, September 2016. The HBR articulation of the JTBD framework. Underpins `templates/jobs-to-be-done-canvas.md` and REFERENCE §2.

- **Christensen, C.M., et al.** *Competing Against Luck.* HarperBusiness, 2016. The full-length JTBD treatment. The four-forces-of-progress framework comes from this work.

- **Klement, A.** *When Coffee and Kale Compete.* NYC Publishing, 2016. Operator-grade JTBD application; particularly useful for early-stage product/marketing fit.

## Strategy and competitive positioning

- **Porter, M.** "How Competitive Forces Shape Strategy." *Harvard Business Review*, March 1979. Five Forces, applied to marketing in REFERENCE §4 (supplier-power view of channel concentration risk).

- **Porter, M.** *Competitive Strategy.* Free Press, 1980. The book-length treatment; particularly the generic-strategies framework (cost leadership / differentiation / focus).

- **Trout, J. & Ries, A.** *Positioning: The Battle for Your Mind.* McGraw-Hill, 1981 (and the 20th-anniversary edition with annotations, 2001). The canonical positioning text. The positioning statement template traces back to this work.

- **Moore, G.** *Crossing the Chasm.* HarperBusiness, 1991/2014. Technology adoption lifecycle and category dynamics for B2B tech. Influences the ICP definition and category-creation frameworks.

- **Kim, W.C. & Mauborgne, R.** *Blue Ocean Strategy.* Harvard Business Review Press, 2004. Category-creation vs category-entry framing; relevant to positioning playbook.

## Behavioral economics and decision-making

- **Kahneman, D.** *Thinking, Fast and Slow.* Farrar, Straus and Giroux, 2011. System 1/2; anchoring, availability, planning fallacy, loss aversion. Every cognitive-bias quick-catch in `heuristics.md` traces back here.

- **Thaler, R. & Sunstein, C.** *Nudge.* Yale University Press, 2008. Choice architecture, defaults, decoy effects — basis for REFERENCE §8 (behavioral pricing).

- **Thaler, R.** *Misbehaving: The Making of Behavioral Economics.* W.W. Norton, 2015. Mental accounting and consumer behavior; informs pricing psychology.

- **Klein, G.** "Performing a Project Premortem." *Harvard Business Review*, September 2007. The premortem method, applied to marketing risk-surfacing in `heuristics.md`.

## SaaS, unit economics, and growth

- **Skok, D.** *forEntrepreneurs* blog (forentrepreneurs.com). The most-cited operator canon on SaaS unit economics, CAC, LTV. Particularly the "SaaS metrics" series and the "Capital Efficient Funnel."

- **McClure, D.** "Startup Metrics for Pirates: AARRR." 500 Startups deck/presentation, 2007 and subsequent iterations. The pirate-metrics funnel framework in REFERENCE §6.

- **Andreessen Horowitz (a16z).** Published essays on SaaS metrics, retention cohorts, and the unit economics stack. Particularly the work of Jeff Jordan, Frank Chen, and Sarah Wang on the operator metric set.

- **OpenView Partners.** SaaS Benchmarks (annual report). Quoted as the source for industry-median LTV/CAC, NRR, CAC payback figures.

## Brand and brand equity

- **Aaker, D.** *Building Strong Brands.* Free Press, 1996. Brand equity framework, brand-as-asset thinking. Underpins REFERENCE §9 (brand equity ladder).

- **Aaker, D. & Joachimsthaler, E.** *Brand Leadership.* Free Press, 2000. The brand-system view; relevant to multi-product portfolio brand questions.

- **Sharp, B.** *How Brands Grow.* Oxford University Press, 2010. The Ehrenberg-Bass tradition — brand growth comes from mental and physical availability. A useful counter-balance to differentiation-only thinking.

## Pricing

- **Ramanujam, M. & Tacke, G.** *Monetizing Innovation.* Wiley, 2016. Value-based pricing; building pricing into product design. Underpins the value-based section of CFO's `pricing-canvas.md`.

- **Anderson, C.** *Free: The Future of a Radical Price.* Hyperion, 2009. Freemium and zero-pricing strategy.

- **Poundstone, W.** *Priceless.* Hill and Wang, 2010. The psychology of pricing — anchoring, decoy effects, the role of context in price perception.

## Channel and distribution

- **Weinberg, G. & Mares, J.** *Traction.* Penguin, 2014. The 19-channel "bullseye framework" for channel exploration. Useful for early-stage channel hypothesis generation.

- **Andreessen, M., Horowitz, B., et al.** a16z blog series on growth and distribution. Particularly the work on "default alive" vs "default dead" and channel diversification strategy.

- **Patel, N.** Various pieces on SEO and content marketing — the operator canon on compounding channel investment.

## Marketing measurement

- **Kaplan, R. & Norton, D.** "The Balanced Scorecard." *Harvard Business Review*, January 1992. The four-perspective measurement framework, generalized for marketing metric design.

- **McKinsey & Company.** Published series on "marketing return on investment" and "marketing performance management." Particularly McKinsey's work on attribution modeling and channel allocation discipline.

- **Bain & Company.** *The Net Promoter System* (Reichheld). The NPS methodology; widely-used customer-loyalty measurement. HBR articles by Frederick Reichheld from 2003 onward.

## Founder/operator marketing perspectives

- **Andrew Chen.** *The Cold Start Problem.* Harper Business, 2021. Network effects and growth dynamics; particularly relevant for PLG and marketplace marketing.

- **April Dunford.** *Obviously Awesome.* Ambient Press, 2019. Operator-grade positioning treatment; how to apply Trout & Ries in 2020s practice.

- **Wes Bush.** *Product-Led Growth.* PLG Collective, 2019. The PLG playbook; relevant to channel allocation in self-serve products.

## A note on sources I would have cited if I could verify URLs

Several specific HBR, McKinsey Quarterly, and Bain Insights articles inform this work. Rather than risk citing dead or wrong URLs, I've cited the *author body of work* and the *publication tradition* — readers can search those at hbr.org, mckinsey.com, bain.com, or via Google Scholar.

The frameworks themselves are stable. The specific articles describing them have proliferated over the decades; the canonical version is usually a book chapter, with the HBR article being the popularizing summary.
