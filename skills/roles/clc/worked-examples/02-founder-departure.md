# Worked example 02 — co-founder departure with IP at issue

**Scenario:** Two co-founders, the company is 14 months old, $400K seed raised. Founder B says they want to leave to "do their own thing." They built half the product's codebase, were never formally on payroll, and the founder agreement was a one-page document that didn't explicitly cover IP assignment. The remaining founder (A) asks: "How do I handle this?"

Walks the most-common founder-stage legal crisis. Shows risk-tier escalation, when to engage counsel, what to preserve, and what NOT to say.

---

## Step 1 — Triage

```
What kind of question?    Multiple — IP assignment, equity / cap table, 
                           potentially employment-legal, possibly fiduciary
Risk tier?                RED.
                           - Co-founder dispute (always RED at minimum)
                           - IP ownership question (RED)
                           - Equity dispute potential (RED)
                           - Possible breach of fiduciary duty exposure (RED)
Jurisdiction?             Wherever the company is incorporated (likely Delaware) 
                           + wherever Founder B resides
Attorney needed?          YES. Immediately. Multiple specialties may apply.
```

**This is unambiguously RED tier.** Stop trying to handle internally. Don't have substantive conversations with Founder B until counsel is engaged.

## Step 2 — Identify the legal questions in play

This isn't one question. It's a cluster:

| Question | Specialist |
|---|---|
| Who owns the code Founder B wrote? | IP / startup corporate attorney |
| What's Founder B's equity vesting status? | Corporate attorney |
| Were they an employee or contractor? | Employment attorney (jurisdiction-specific) |
| What does the founder agreement actually say? | Corporate attorney review of the document |
| Are there 83(b) election issues? | Tax + corporate |
| What disclosure / consent does the board need? | Corporate / fiduciary |
| Are investor rights triggered (board notification, drag-along, etc.)? | Corporate / securities |
| How do we communicate this to the team? | Communications + employment |

**No single attorney covers all of these.** Operator move: engage a startup-specialist corporate attorney FIRST. They'll triangulate to the IP and employment specialists if needed.

## Step 3 — What Founder A should do this week

```
HOUR 0 (today)

☐ Stop substantive conversations with Founder B. Be cordial and human, but 
   defer specifics ("Let's set up a call next week to talk about transition; 
   I want to make sure we handle this thoughtfully.")
☐ Document Founder B's stated reasons in your own notes (not shared)
☐ Locate and review the founder agreement, equity grant documents, any 
   IP-assignment language, and the cap table
☐ Engage a startup-specialist corporate attorney TODAY
☐ Issue an internal document hold (limited to those who know — likely just 
   Founder A initially) on emails, Slack, code repositories, design files 
   related to Founder B's work

HOUR 1-24

☐ Counsel call to walk through the situation
☐ Identify the immediate priorities (typically: secure IP, preserve docs, 
   plan disclosure to investors)
☐ Decide what to tell the rest of the team and when

WEEK 1

☐ Counsel-drafted transition / separation agreement reflecting:
   - IP assignment of everything Founder B has touched
   - Resolution of equity / vesting (typically: only vested shares stay; 
     unvested may be repurchased or accelerated)
   - Confidentiality + non-disparagement (mutual)
   - Release of claims (mutual)
   - Severance / consideration if any
☐ Investor notification (per investor rights agreement obligations)
☐ Board action documenting the departure and cap-table changes
☐ Team communication (counsel-approved messaging)
```

## Step 4 — The IP-assignment problem specifically

This is the highest-stakes issue.

**Default rule (US):** the person who creates code, designs, content, etc., owns the IP unless explicitly assigned.

**If the founder agreement didn't include IP assignment language:**
- Founder B may legally own significant portions of the product
- This is exactly the leverage that turns "amicable departure" into "negotiated settlement"
- The longer this is unresolved, the more leverage Founder B has

**Operator move (counsel-driven):**
- Counsel reviews the founder agreement carefully — sometimes IP-assignment language is implicit or in unexpected sections
- If genuinely missing, counsel structures the transition / separation agreement to include retroactive IP assignment AS A CONDITION of receiving any consideration (severance, accelerated vesting, mutual release)
- Founder B may resist; counsel negotiates

**What NOT to do:**
- Don't tell Founder B "your code is the company's" without paper backing it
- Don't quietly remove their commit access until counsel-approved
- Don't make a public announcement until the IP question is resolved in writing
- Don't try to negotiate the IP assignment yourself; counsel-only

## Step 5 — The equity / vesting problem

Typical founder vesting: 4 years with 1-year cliff. At 14 months, Founder B likely has:
- ~30% of their granted equity vested (assuming standard 4-year vesting)
- ~70% unvested, which the company usually has the right to repurchase at original purchase price

**What counsel typically does:**
- Verify exact vesting schedule and current status
- Confirm whether the company elected vesting (typical) or not
- Determine repurchase rights for unvested shares
- Calculate cap table impact
- Plan whether to repurchase, leave unvested, or negotiate

**Pitfalls counsel will flag:**
- 83(b) election issues (if Founder B filed an 83(b), they paid tax on the original grant value; repurchase implications differ)
- Acceleration triggers (some founder agreements have acceleration on certain events; check carefully)
- ROFR (right of first refusal) on any vested shares Founder B might want to sell
- Investor "drag-along" rights if applicable

## Step 6 — The communication question

When and how do you tell:
- The rest of the company
- Investors
- Customers
- The public

**Sequencing (counsel-approved):**

1. **Counsel finalizes the separation agreement with Founder B.** Don't disclose externally before this is signed.
2. **Notify investors per the investor rights agreement.** Typically a specific board update or written notification within X days.
3. **Notify the team internally** with counsel-approved messaging. Typically all-hands meeting. Limit details to what's necessary.
4. **Update external materials** (website, About page, LinkedIn) only after internal communication.
5. **Customer-facing communication** only if directly relevant (e.g., if Founder B was the relationship owner for a major customer).

**What NOT to say (counsel-approved messaging usually excludes):**
- Reasons for departure (unless mutually agreed)
- Negative characterizations of either party
- Specific financial / equity terms
- Speculation about future actions by Founder B

Mutual non-disparagement clauses in the separation agreement should be enforced — by both sides.

## Step 7 — Investor obligations

Most seed and Series A docs include obligations like:

- Notify investors of co-founder departure within X days
- Provide reasonable detail (without breaching the separation agreement)
- Board approval of changes to cap table
- Sometimes investor consent rights are triggered

**Operator move:** counsel reviews the investor rights agreement and identifies all triggered obligations BEFORE the company takes action. Surprising investors with a co-founder departure they learn about from LinkedIn is the worst possible scenario.

## Step 8 — The recommendation

```
Routing: CLC (primary). CFO weighs in on the cap table and financial 
implications. CHRO weighs in on the team communication and employment 
status issues.

Risk tier: RED.

Do not engage Founder B substantively before counsel is in the loop. Be 
cordial, defer specifics ("Let's set up a call next week — I want to make 
sure we handle this thoughtfully and fairly"), and use the time to secure 
your position.

The highest-stakes issue is IP. If the founder agreement doesn't include 
explicit IP-assignment language, Founder B may legally own significant 
portions of the product they built. This is exactly the leverage that turns 
"amicable departure" into "negotiated settlement." The longer this is 
unresolved, the more expensive it gets to resolve.

Immediate moves:

1. Today: engage a startup-specialist corporate attorney. Send them the 
   founder agreement, equity grant documents, current cap table, and a 
   one-page summary of the situation. Budget $2,500-5,000 for the initial 
   engagement.

2. Today: issue an internal document hold. Don't delete anything related 
   to Founder B's work — emails, Slack, code repositories, design files.

3. This week: counsel will help structure a separation agreement that 
   makes IP assignment retroactive AND a condition of receiving any 
   consideration (severance, accelerated vesting, mutual release).

4. Don't tell anyone else about the departure yet, except in narrowly-defined 
   need-to-know cases (probably nobody until the separation agreement is 
   signed).

5. Don't reduce Founder B's commit access, system access, or visibility 
   without counsel approval. Premature lockdown signals dispute and gives 
   them leverage.

Tradeoff: this approach costs you $5,000-15,000 in counsel fees over the 
next 30 days. It also feels uncomfortably formal for what may be an amicable 
departure. The alternative — handling informally without counsel — has 
catastrophic downside if the IP question goes badly. The downside of 
over-formalizing is much smaller than the downside of under-formalizing.

What to watch:
- Founder B's behavior over the next 2-4 weeks. If they start removing files, 
  forking the repo to personal accounts, contacting investors directly, or 
  posting publicly — escalate to litigation counsel.
- Investor reaction when notified. Most are professional about this; some 
  will overreact. Manage expectations.
- Team reaction once internally communicated. Plan for at least 1-2 follow-up 
  resignations; co-founder departures often trigger them.

When to escalate to litigation counsel (RED-RED):
- Founder B refuses to sign a reasonable separation agreement
- Founder B starts taking actions inconsistent with their fiduciary duties 
  (still a director / officer of the company)
- Founder B claims IP rights inconsistent with company's claims
- Founder B contacts customers, investors, or media without authorization
- Anything that smells like coordination with a competitor or new venture 
  built on company materials

When to call a different specialist:
- Tax attorney if 83(b) election issues surface
- Employment attorney if Founder B's status (employee vs. contractor) is 
  genuinely ambiguous
- Securities attorney if the cap-table changes trigger filing obligations

This is a moment where counsel earns its keep. The cost of getting this 
right is in the four figures. The cost of getting it wrong is in the six 
to seven figures.

Want me to file an engage-mode memo capturing this analysis for your records?
```

## Step 9 — What made this answer good

| Dimension | Why it works |
|---|---|
| Risk-tier classified as RED with explicit reasoning | Founder-IP-equity cluster always RED |
| Names multiple specialists needed | Corporate → IP → employment → tax → securities |
| Surfaces the hidden leverage point (IP) | Most founders don't realize the IP gap until counsel surfaces it |
| Concrete sequencing (hour-by-week) | Don't escalate prematurely, but don't delay either |
| Names what NOT to do | Lockdown, premature disclosure, informal IP "agreement" |
| Communication discipline framed | Counsel-approved messaging, sequenced disclosure |
| Investor obligations flagged | Easy thing to forget that becomes its own legal issue |
| Cross-role honesty | CFO + CHRO + CLC all in play |
| Budget transparency | $2,500-5,000 initial counsel; $5,000-15,000 total over 30 days |

## Step 10 — What the bad version would look like

A weaker CLC response:

> "Co-founder departures are tricky. You should probably talk to a lawyer. 
> The main thing is to be fair — Founder B helped build the company. Try to 
> have an honest conversation about what they want, and you can probably 
> work something out amicably. Just don't let it drag on."

What's wrong:
- Doesn't classify risk tier
- "Talk to a lawyer" without specifying which kind
- Doesn't surface the IP-assignment gap (the highest-stakes issue)
- Doesn't sequence the moves
- Misframes — "be fair" and "honest conversation" before legal protection are exactly the moves that lose you the IP question
- Doesn't name what NOT to do

The CLC role's value in RED-tier situations is naming the specific moves with the specific risks. Hedging in RED situations causes real damage.

## Variants

| If… | Then… |
|---|---|
| The founder agreement DOES explicitly assign IP | Massive risk reduction. Still call counsel, but the conversation is much simpler. |
| Founder B is leaving for a clear personal reason (family, health, geography) with no competitive concern | Lower-risk version of the same process, but still RED until separation agreement signed |
| Founder B is leaving to start a directly competing business | RED-RED. Litigation counsel involvement likely. Non-compete enforceability becomes critical (jurisdiction-specific). |
| The company has multiple co-founders staying | Easier on team communication; harder on cap table dynamics (the staying founders' equity may shift) |
| Investors are aggressive types (e.g., one of the big VC firms) | They'll want immediate detailed notification. Plan accordingly with counsel. |
| You're at <6 months runway | Counsel + a more compressed timeline. Sometimes Founder B's leverage is reduced if the company is genuinely at risk. |
| Founder B is the technical co-founder (CTO) and is essential to the product | Operational continuity becomes the dominant issue. May require staged transition with paid consultation period. |
